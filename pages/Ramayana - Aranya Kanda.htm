Aranya Kanda

 

Prelude:

 

After a year and a half in Chitrakoota’s charming environment

Rama moves into Dandaka, replete with nature’s nourishment

He, with Sita and Lakshmana, lives there for over a decade

Visiting great ascetics whose wisdom surges forth like a cascade

Afterwards, Rama camps at Panchavati for two years

Sita gets kidnapped then: it is time for piteous tears

 

The Story in Detail:

 

On quitting Chitrakoota, Rama and His companions walked ahead

They came upon a gigantic monster whose form inspired dread

The colossus, brushing aside the brothers, grabbed Sita and fled

Rama and Lakshmana gave chase and climbed up to the giant’s head

When hacking him yielded no significant result

The clever and brave princes, with each other, began to consult

 

Lakshmana slid down and dug a large pit

The monster, kicked hard by Rama, fell, heavily hit

Quickly pushing him into the hole, Rama filled it

An incandescent person rose from there and spoke words, fit

“O Rama, I have heard your lotus feet praised

From a long-standing curse, I know, Ahalya They raised.

 

Your immortal feet alone are able to grant deliverance

From the birth-death cycle, which sorely tests one's forbearance

In material life, a mother and child recognise one another readily

Though You understand us, why don’t we know the mother in You, easily?  

Today, I, Viradha, have seen the power of Your feet first-hand

I thank and salute thee, as before my eyes, You charmingly stand.

 

Kubera cursed me for hankering after his woman:

I have been wandering this earth ever since as a demon.

Being Sriman Narayana, the Supreme Being

It is inexplicable why You hide in man’s garb, an exiled king!”

Rama then replied: “O Viradha, I am pleased by your supplication

Go towards your heavenly abode and acquire your designation.”

 

On Viradha’s advice, Rama reached the precincts of Sarabhanga’s hermitage

He went forward to announce their visit to that aged sage

Near the entrance, He perceived Indra’s chariot and Airavath, his elephant white

Stopping, He heard Indra and Sarabhanga conversing, with a smile quiet 

Indra was putting forth an offer, “O sage, your austerities have been severe

They make you eligible for heaven—believe my words, sincere.”

 

Sarabhanga replied, “Indra, my desire is to reach a place eternal:

A point of no return, where the soul experiences pleasures outside the carnal

Neither your world nor even the ‘Satyaloka’ of ‘Brahma’ are places permanent

In Narayana’s everlasting abode, Sri Vaikunta, I hope to become an ornament

Sri Rama will be here soon and upon me cast His compassionate eye

Then my sins and good deeds alike shall vanish and at His eternal feet, I shall lie.”

 

Indra soon took leave of Sarabhanga and stepped out

He said to Rama: “You are the beginning and culmination, no doubt.” 

After Indra left, Sarabhanga invited Rama and His mates eagerly

And fulfilled a host's obligations towards guests, tenderly

Said he, “I have meditated on You for a thousand years and more

And waited for Your peerless lotus feet to reach this door.”

 

Sarabhanga then sought Rama’s permission to enter fire

And explained: “It is but an excuse to transcend this physical mire.”

Saying thus, the ancient and venerable ‘yogi’ and sage

Jumped into fire and sacrificed his earthly bondage

Fate now propelled Rama, His beloved wife, and Lakshmana harder 

He had to fulfil His destiny of killing Ravana and establish order.

 

Now, Sutheekshana's audience, was what Rama sought 

That sagacious hermit received Rama with humility, as he ought

After plying the visitors with sufficient water and food 

Sutheekshana requested Rama to remain with him, for good 

But, Rama though grateful, would not comply 

"We cannot disturb the harmony of this place" was His reply. 

 

After staying the night, Rama took the sage's permission 

Sutheekshana gave leave and reminded Rama about His mission: 

"The demons are ravaging this area with their heinous acts

We are unable to perform our sacred duties: these are facts.

We rely on You, the supreme saviour, to come to our rescue 

Annihilate them: that is the course you must pursue." 

 

Then, Rama, accompanied by Sita and Lakshmana went deeper into the forest

And saw many huts amidst dense trees that housed saints truest

Those residents waited to place the atrocities against them in Rama’s view

One of them yelled, “Rama is close—look how the trees reflect His divine hue!”

Catching sight of Rama, those recluses with hearts unsullied

Forgot their pain and to bless Him, forward they hurried.

 

“Live thee long, dear King, with Your consort and queen

May no harm come upon You! May Your glory ever remain keen!”

Devotees steeped in love for the Lord always place

His wellbeing ahead of theirs, however hard the situation they face

Their love blinds even their boundless wisdom:

They forget He is the omnipotent absolute of every Kingdom

 

The devoted sages of Dandaka too blessed the Supreme for their part:

If He is well, fortunate remains creation, His counterpart 

Rama and His companions bowed as a mark of their respect

Beholding the sages’ perturbed faces, Rama began to introspect

“Have I failed to learn their troubles and protect them?” He wondered

“If so, I have been remiss—and have shamefully blundered.”

 

By patiently questioning and eliciting answers from each pious being

The unpardonable crimes of the demons, Rama began seeing

“I should long ere have come to you and wiped your tears

I blush like a woman for failing to abate your fears

Do not worry, dear souls. I make a solemn promise to you all

I shall destroy every demon acting against Dharma: from today, that is my call.”

 

The threesome then wended farther into the Dandaka jungles

Where Rama met several more sages and carefully heard out their troubles.

Sita raised a doubt: “How can You wage war on someone without direct provocation?”

Rama explained, “As Ayodhya’s royal agent, defending citizens is my vocation.”

That dutiful wife and universal mother then felt placated in her heart

“My husband will wipe the suffering of our devoted children and play His part.” 

 

Rama, Sita, and Lakshmana soaked in the natural beauty

That Dandaka offered, along with its pristine bounty

Looming mountains, clear lakes, and gently flowing rivers captivated

While fruits, roots and forest produce had them satiated

The daily interactions with holy men and the living close to nature

Exposed the three to an intimate and intense spiritual culture

 

After passing close to ten years in a blissfully beneficent way,

Rama returned to Sutheekshana's hermitage for another short stay

He expressed His wish to meet Sage Agastya, the pious and kindly soul

Sutheekshana, overjoyed, got ready to play the guide's role

"Agastya ‘muni’ has made this part of the forest safe for the likes of us

He protects us through his penance and powers, without a fuss."

 

Following Sutheekshana’s directions, Rama soon reached the dwelling of a sage

"Behold the hut of the brother of Agastya, who can quell any wicked rage.

Vataapi and Ilvala were two 'rakshasas' tormenting pious folks here

Agastya digested the former and burnt the latter, with his ascetic prowess, sheer."

Saying thus, Rama entered Agastya's brother's residence 

And bowed to that sage who shone with spiritual effulgence.

 

After staying a night at the hermitage of Agastya's brother

Rama, keen to meet Agastya, took leave of His host and walked farther.

Soon, He, Janaki and Lakshmana, entered a place filled with ethereal calm

"Just stepping into this place feels like a soothing balm

This must be the exalted Sage Agastya's home

Where even fierce creatures without animosity roam."

 

"Lakshmana, by his mere presence the holy man has this place sanctified

His gentle kindness and mercy cannot be adequately glorified.

Please make haste and enter his hut to announce our arrival

Eager for his advice, I can hardly wait, even for a short interval."

Presently, Agastya, the omniscient sage with fame fathomless 

Stepped forth and welcomed Sri Rama with elation, boundless.

 

The sage presented them with refreshments, being hospitable

And then narrated to Rama the demons' brutalities, most despicable

Gathering Rama's intentions, Agastya showed approval

And said: "Here are Vishnu's weapons; they must be at Your disposal."

He then handed over a powerful bow, two self-replenishing quivers and a sword:

“May these bring victory in your impending battles to establish 'Dharmic' code!"

 

Then the trio spent the night with Agastya and his many disciples 

Upon waking, Rama made plans aligned with just principles

He then took Sita and Lakshmana to nearby 'Panchavati' at Agastya's behest

The sage had predicted: "For future events to take shape, that location would be best.

Injustice, wherever it exists, is a threat to justice everywhere

You’re poised to uproot evil and bring peace: of this, I’m aware.”

 

Walking towards Panchavati, Rama and His companions came upon a mammoth vulture

Rama approached the bird, suspecting it to be a demonic creature 

But, the mighty bird spoke in a sweet voice: “Dear boy Rama, I am your father’s friend.

I am glad to welcome You here and shall look after Your welfare from my end.”

The brothers and Sita became happy at finding a well-wisher in the remote spot

Rama thought of him as His father, whose loss had made Him distraught 

 

Jatayu then proceeded to narrate the history of creation 

He spoke about the ancestry of animals, birds and humans without hesitation

“I am the offspring of Aruna, the charioteer of the Sun

I have an elder brother, Sampati, who has for me, much affection.

You are my beloved friend’s son and I feel responsible

For protecting you all from predators and demons terrible.”

 

Panchavati, on the banks of River Godavari, proved an enchanting place

Where every prospect pleased and of evil, there was apparently no trace.

Rama found a suitable spot for their stay and instructed:

“Dear brother, raise a residence that cannot be easily destructed.

Build a charming hermitage where Sita and I would delight

Let it also be a place that gratifies you and makes your heart light."

 

Hearing Rama's words, Lakshmana shot at Him a look of alarm

"Have you decided to abandon me?” asked he, the one with the steady arm

A chagrined Rama questioned: "What have I said to cause you this anxiety?

I only directed you to serve me, don't I well know your piety?"

Lakshmana replied: "One must act only to gladden Your heart

At Your delight, one must rejoice, and from this rule I never depart"

 

Lakshmana continued, "I don’t entertain a single independent thought

I shall always depend on and look unto you, no matter what."

Hearing these words of acumen, Rama was overjoyed

And exclaimed, "When you're beside me, I always feel buoyed"

Lakshmana then quickly raised a ‘Parna Shala’ highly captivating:

For Rama and Sita’s solitude, He had designed a room, enchanting.

 

Rama carefully inspected the abode Lakshmana had erected

He saw several beautiful rooms that His brother’s skills reflected

Rama had wanted a private chamber, but had felt shy to say so:

Noticing the secluded room Lakshmana had built, He uttered a grateful “Oh!”

“Soumitri, you fulfil my wishes like my own father

About stating them explicitly, I never have to bother.”

 

A soul that wishes to serve the supreme must follow Lakshmana's example:

One must think about what would please the master, without preamble.

Any action that appeals to the super soul is 'Dharmic' and just

Anything that causes pain to Him is sinful and unjust

It is essential to note: when a person acts from this perspective,

The ego is diminished and all his actions become constructive

 

The three began their sojourn at Panchavati on a peaceful note

The place had donned nature’s exquisite and plentiful coat 

One day at dawn, Rama and Lakshmana with a view to taking a bath

Headed for the Godavari river and strolled along their customary path

On entering the waters, the brothers shivered in the cold

In Lakshmana’s mind, Bharatha’s torment began to unfold

 

Turning to Rama, that affectionate brother said: “This water is so chill.

How much icier it must be for Bharatha, our brother with a strong will?

He bathes in the Sarayu, in the wee hours of the dawn

Wishing to stay well away from our citizens’ scorn

He is reluctant to hear them say: “Here goes the sinner

Who separated from us that noble Rama, our life-giver!”

 

Listening to Lakshmana’s words, Rama was seized by an emotional frenzy

“I am returning to Ayodhya this instant,” said He, working Himself into a tizzy

“Enough of keeping My word to our father! 

I cannot stay away from Bharatha any longer.

Whether you concur with me or not, I shall not consider

I shall unite with my brother and remain at his side forever.”

 

It was not in Rama’s nature to retract from His vow

His blinding love for Bharatha His response did but show 

Lakshmana, taken aback, wondered how to restore Rama’s equanimity

He quickly started talking about Kaikeyi and her sudden decline from amity

At this Rama stopped him and begged: “Don’t cast aspersions on our mother

After all, she is the one who yielded us Bharatha, that treasure-like brother.”

 

In truth, however, Bharatha, excessively feverish from the agony of separation

Never felt the chill of Sarayu's waters in his affliction

Like a freshly baked brick that is dipped in cold water makes the water hot

With Sarayu’s cold waters, Bharatha’s emotions passionately fought

Every single day for fourteen years, that epitome of devotion

Carried Rama in his heart on a throne of unwavering adoration

 

To Rama, though Lakshmana was near, Bharatha was dear

Any indication of pain to him stabbed at Him like a spear

Lakshmana’s timely distraction brought Rama back to His duty:

His promise of shelter given to the ‘sadhus’ was His treaty.

This incident, however, brings forth the depth of His feeling:

Where it concerns His devotee, Rama is not above losing His bearing  

 

Passing their time pleasantly on Panchavati’s verdant lap

Rama, Sita, and Lakshmana entered the last year of exile without mishap

But, as fated, trouble arrived in the form of Shurpanaka, the demoness 

She perceived Rama, and enamoured by His looks, craved His caresses

Though Rama and Shurpanaka were opposites in character and attractiveness

Shurpanaka became a prisoner to lust and sensuousness.

 

Rama’s face was like a lotus bloom while Shurpanaka’s was unsightly

He had a slender and taut waist whereas she was hefty and ungainly

Rama’s hair coiled like a sinuous black cobra while Shurpanaka’s was bleached

His voice was sweet as nectar whereas hers jarred as she screeched!

To create a favourable impression, Shurpanaka decided on deceit

An idea hatched in her mind, loathsome in spirit

 

Changing into a comely maiden, she approached Rama and queried:

“Who are you? To you, who shine like a divine being, I wish to get married.” 

Rama introduced Himself as the son of Dasaratha, Ayodhya’s emperor

And narrated the events that culminated in His exile with His wife and brother

He then enquired: “Now, will you tell me about yourself?”

Shurpanaka replied: “I am the sister of the demon King Ravana himself.

 

She continued, “I have fallen for your charms and long to make you mine

Marry me, O Rama, struck as I am by you, who is brighter than moonshine.”

Smilingly Rama said: “I am a married man; here you see Sita, my wife

I have vowed that I shall not even gaze upon another woman in my life.

Why don’t you ask my brother Lakshmana who stands here?

Though he too is married, his wife is not near.”

 

To pair up with the equally handsome Lakshmana, Shurpanaka was not averse

She said to him: "Marry me; together, many lovely places we shall traverse.”

Lakshmana rejected her offer and said: “I am Rama’s slave and nothing more.

A lady like you deserves none but the one at the fore.”

Accepting his decree, the demoness went back to Rama with her request

Once again Rama declined and directed her to Lakshmana in jest

 

When the brothers treated Shurpanaka thus, she lost her patience

And grabbing and throttling Sita, she gave vent to her grievance

Promptly, Rama motioned to Lakshmana with His eyes

Who slashed away Shurpanaka’s nose and ears in a trice

The demoness let out an agonised roar that shook the very ground

And ran helter-skelter with fury in her breasts that stood up like a mound

 

Shurpanaka headed straight for Janasthana inhabited by Khara, her sibling

Collapsing at his feet in a pool of blood, she began raving and ranting:

“Handsome youths named Rama and Lakshmana have insulted me thus

Though gentle, they can handle even powerful demons without a fuss.

Rama has large eyes like a couple of lotuses lovely

The youth, clad in dark deer-skin, appear lively."

 

Shurpanaka’s praise confirms the impression the Supreme makes on every creature

His attributes fascinate even the wickedest—that is the diktat of nature

Then recollecting herself, Shurpanaka gave further details of the brothers 

And warned Khara about their intent and the inherent dangers.

“We cannot allow them to continue here—they pose a risk for our kind

They are sure to destroy us, who love to be unjust and unkind.”

 

“The princes of Ayodhya wield powerful bows and are well versed in battle arts

Before they assault us, you must rout them out from these parts

Sita, that voluptuous beauty, is Rama’s wife and dearer to Him than life

When I tried gobbling her, Lakshmana chopped me with his sharp knife.

Unless and until I see Sita’s blood, I shall not know any peace

Only after I eat her and the royal brothers, my fury shall cease.”

 

Khara dispatched his formidable assistants, who numbered twelve 

Certain they would kill the humans, his concern he did shelve

But, when Shurpanaka brought back news that the demons had perished at Rama's hand

Khara sent his brother Dushana with fourteen thousand more from his select band

At the advent of the huge army, even the Gods appeared: 

One against fourteen thousand was appalling and weird.

 

Rama stood like a rock while the demons descended on Him like waves

His countenance shone with a smiling light that scared those knaves

Rama then raised His bow and began releasing sharp arrows 

The sky became thick with His flying missiles, chilling the demons' marrows

All of a sudden, Rama seemed to disappear from His place

He discharged arrows with such speed that He became a blur without a face.

 

Within a few hours, the invincible Rama decimated that army's force

And as He stood, the blood pouring forth from Him resembled a river’s course

Khara, shocked by the defeat of his mates, met Rama in face-to-face combat

Very soon, his spirit left for the realms it deserved, while his body lay flat

The heavenly denizens rained happy flowers in profusion

And praised the Raghu scion's prodigious feat in effusion 

 

Sita commended Rama thus: “Lord of lords of infinite virtue

You have established Dharma, which the demons eternally eschew

Were my father to see You this day, He would feel proud

About having given my hand to a man whose valour speaks aloud.

Let me tend to Your wounds from the battle

Every one of them represents justice and shows Your mettle.”

 

The elated Sita ran up to Her heroic husband and embraced Him fondly

Her touch that held the power of all medicinal herbs, healed Him magically

That day, Akhampana was the only demon that got away from death 

Hidden as he was in woman's apparel, Rama had not snuffed out his breath

This demon and Shurpanaka now fled to Ravana in a dead fright:

They had to warn their king about Rama and His indefatigable might 

 

Akhampana informed Ravana about Khara-Dushana’s defeat

That king forgot to enquire how that battle their army could forfeit

“When every one of Khara’s demons is overthrown and dead,

Explain how you are here” Ravana wondered, instead.

Akhampana, whose name meant ‘the one who never trembles’

Boldly praised Rama thus: “I posed as an inoffensive lady, knowing cruelty alone Rama humbles.” 

 

Next Shurpanaka came forward with her mutilated features and told her story

To entice her brother, she cunningly described first Sita’s beauty and glory

Then she said, “Though Rama and Lakshmana are mere humans

It is inexplicable that their powers should be far superior to us demons

Better to trim their hostile onslaught at the outset

By harming Sita, you can damage Rama’s spirit and have Him upset”

 

“A sorrowful and broken Rama is unlikely to put up a fight

Melancholy will rob Him of all His strength and make him contrite.”

Cruel minds always seek and succumb to bad advice

Here too Shurpanaka’s words appealed to Ravana, the unwise.

He foolishly thought of fingering Rama, that tiger among men

And decided upon kidnapping Sita and bringing Her to his den.

 

Driven by lust and fear, Ravana went to meet Mareecha, his uncle

Who was a cunning trickster and of every atrocity, capable

But, the moment Ravana mentioned Rama, that hardened demon turned ashen

And in a low voice asked: “Who gave this idea that would your death fashion?”

When a person’s end is near, his mind stoops to horrific deeds

Fate propels him towards doom and to the best of wisdom, he seldom heeds

 

Ravana, piqued, demanded the reason for Mareecha’s dread

That demon explained why in Rama’s path it was useless to tread

“Many years ago, when with Rama I was fool enough to fight 

That mere boy, with His bow and arrows, put me to flight

Flung infinitely far from His presence, I recognised His power:

It can only be the Supreme’s, against which every one must cower.” 

 

“Know Rama not as a human, but as the ultimate Lord Himself

Keep away from Him, if you want to save your kinsfolk and yourself.”

Thus did Mareecha, intrepidly counsel Ravana, the malevolent being

But, his advice fell on deaf ears, and Ravana remained unseeing

Harshly he spoke: “I want you to kidnap Sita by using your tricks

If you dare defy me, I shall squish you like a few ticks.”

 

Poor Mareecha realised that his death awaited him

And decided to die at Rama’s hands rather than at Ravana’s whim

He said: “I see there is no point in warning you

To invite death, meddling with the divine couple Rama and Sita is the perfect cue.

Turning myself into a golden deer, I shall in front of Sita prance

And tempt Her to ask Rama for me as a pet: I well know the nuance.”

 

“You will hide nearby in the guise of a holy Sage

While the noble Rama, I shall lure far away and engage

Eventually, Rama will strike me down with His sharp arrow

I shall yell for Lakshmana’s help and for that Rama’s voice I shall borrow

When the coast is thus fully clear, in front of Sita, you shall appear

And snatching Her quickly, you shall return: do you hear?”

 

Ravana applauded Mareecha’s plan and agreed to go with it

“Let us act immediately; for bold people, procrastinating is unfit”

Mareecha instantly changed into a wondrous deer with a golden tint

His body had jewel-like spots—of shiny gems they gave a hint

He frolicked and frisked enticingly in front of Rama’s hut

Sita longed to have it as a pet and watched it strut.

 

Soon, She turned to Rama and said: “Please capture that deer 

It will be company and live with us forever without fear.”

Rama was determined to fulfil His wife’s desire

He left to seize that deer, unaware that it was akin to playing with fire

Lakshmana stopped Rama and said: “This deer is a fake.

We have neither heard of nor seen this sort of beast in our wake.

 

Also, look at how the other deer in the herd keep away from it

It is a demon transformed: don’t give chase, use Your wit.”

But, Rama consoled Lakshmana thus: “Don’t be anxious

I am eager to please Sita—of this stag, she is desirous.”

Asking Lakshmana to guard Sita, Rama went near that stag

The animal ran a short distance and looked back—He was not one to lag

 

Rama ran after the deer but it remained just at arm’s length

After a long while, He realised that Lakshmana’s case had strength

Releasing a powerful arrow, He slay that enchanted deer

As it fell, it yelled “hey Sita, hey Lakshmana”—inducing in Rama a nameless fear.

The astute Rama realised that it must be Ravana's redressal 

For Khara-Dushana's death, He was facing a reprisal

 

Rama wondered: “Why did this demon shout in my voice?

I sense danger and must hurry back instead of losing my poise.”

While Rama was thinking thus, Sita turned paranoid

And asked Lakshmana to seek Rama—of Her senses becoming devoid.

“Your elder brother is in peril and cries for your assistance

You must rush; don’t stand here and put up resistance.”

 

But Lakshmana refusing to budge, tried placating his sister-in-law

“No one can harm Rama, the Lord of all creation,” he reassured without a flaw

Sita, however, was incensed and began uttering words unfair:

To say such words against that pure-souled Lakshmana, none would dare

“You want to see your brother dead and are not to be trusted

 You perhaps also hanker for me: your moral code has rusted.”

 

Unable to bear Sita’s accusations and piercing taunts

Lakshmana reluctantly decided to give in to her wants

He drew a line of guard, a ‘Lakshman Rekha’, around their hut

And begged Sita to always stay within that 

He then prayed to all the elements of nature:

“Please safeguard Sita: I am uneasy about the future.”

 

Lakshmana then set out from there at a run

To seek Rama, that invincible ornament of the dynasty of the Sun

In a trice, the evil Ravana who had been hiding, revealed himself

He was disguised as a ‘sadhu’ to conceal his true self 

“Bhavathi bhiksham dehi,” that horrid creature cried

Asking for alms, like any real sage, and of retribution, unafraid.

 

Sita came out of the hut and bowed before the demon king

And went in to fetch food worthy of a sadhu’s offering 

When she tried to drop the alms into the bowl Ravana held

That unworthy creature stepped back—in his eyes anger welled

He said: “How dare you serve me from within your home’s precinct?

You lack etiquette and fail to esteem exalted souls: that is distinct.”

 

“I shall proceed to curse you dreadfully and also your clan

Unless you step out and relieve my hunger that is turning me wan.”

Alas, that innocent princess, fell for the trickster’s ruse

And stepped over Lakshmana’s line, to stop the sage’s abuse

Ravana, tearing off his mask, stood revealed as a monster

Sita swooned away and fell to the ground without a bolster.

 

Ravana uprooted and lifted the ground on which Sita lay 

And gloating over his burden, began flying away 

Sita woke to find herself in the demon’s clutch

And began wailing for help: “Recuse me from this wretch.

Oh, trees, flowers, deer and peacocks, tell my Rama what occurred

Oh, river Godavari, narrate this abduction by Ravana, the thief and coward.”

 

When Ravana began flying higher, Sita felt helpless 

Her cries for help fell on the ears of Jatayu, that giant bird selfless

Jatayu came with outstretched wings and clawed at Ravana valiantly

The demon took his ‘Chandrahasa’ sword and chopped down his wings derisively

The poor bird fell to earth and lay in a death swoon

And felt forlorn about abandoning Sita, whose voice paralleled a bird’s croon

 

Sita, horrified by Jatayu's plight, started mourning aloud

"I committed two sins: my life they have begun to cloud.

Having Rama, who represents everything next to me, I hankered for a deer

And I flung words at Lakshmana that were hideous and hurtful to hear

For the former and latter, I shall have two sufferings: one small and one vast

The Lord forgives insults to Himself but alas, never to a devotee who is steadfast."

 

Now, Ravana tried luring Sita with riches and a position of his empress

The pure lady shunned them and said: "Vanity, lust and greed, the wise repress.

You do not realise that you have inserted your arm into a venomous snake's lair

Never doubt it: My beloved Lord Rama will punish you and take back His wife, fair.

If you have an iota of sense, recognise the impending peril

Handing me respectfully over to Rama is the only way to escape his ill-will."

 

"You have grievously injured Jatayu, who was to the three of us a father

And you stole me away sneakily like a craven and a robber

I am a 'pathi-vratha' who is faithful to her husband in mind, word, and deed

Before submitting to you or your riches, I'll give up my very breath, indeed."

Sita's bold admonition infuriated Ravana, the mean fellow with a black heart

And he started flying higher and faster: advice to him, none could impart!

 

Sita perceived a group of pious-looking monkeys on a lush mountain peak

Removing her jewels and tying them in her scarf, she dropped them like a streak

"Please let these show Rama the route in which I am being taken

And convey my courage and conviction to Him, who must be thoroughly shaken.

May these monkeys hand over to my husband the jewel pouch!

May they remember to mention my forced flight and my present safety, vouch."

 

Ravana, provoked by Sita's reprimands, failed to notice her clever act

And the brave princess' faith in Her husband, which nothing could impact.

The demon king now flew over an ocean, verily intimidating

But, Sita who had Rama in Her heart, remained unflinching 

Ravana said, landing in his kingdom Lanka at a spot beauty-laden:

"Till you consent to be my wife, you shall remain a prisoner in this Asoka garden."

 

Meanwhile, Rama who was returning, and Lakshmana met each other

Rama said: “Sita is in danger; you should have held your post, dear brother.” 

The princes rushed back with fear chilling their hearts

Their presentiment came true and an empty hut hit them like darts

Running hither and thither, the brothers looked for Sita and cried out her name

When they couldn’t find her, Rama knew not whom to blame.

 

In His welter of emotions, Rama felt the world whirling round and round

Distracted and dizzy, that ever-upright spirit slid to the ground 

Lakshmana sat next to his brother and calming him spoke thus:

“Rama, this is no time to give in to melancholic or pessimistic fuss

We must move forward and gather all possible clues

And learn Sita’s whereabouts: hence, shed Your blues.”

 

Rama sat up swiftly and announced with a face diffused with anger:

“If the Gods fail to return my Sita today, I shall make the earth stagger.” 

Lakshmana consoled Him again and soon the brothers were on their feet 

And went looking for the lost lady, Rama’s sweet and eternal helpmeet

Presently they stumbled upon Jatayu lying in a pool of blood

Rama rushed to him with tears pouring down in a flood.

 

“O mighty bird, why do you lie thus, fainting and prostrate?

Who attacked you so pitilessly: to learn this I am desperate.”

Jatayu slowly opened his eyes and perceived Rama’s anxious face

“May thee live long, dear son,” said he, before narrating his case

“I heard Sita’s screams and saw Ravana fly south bearing Her away

Realising You must be far, I tried to stop him till You could come this way.”

 

“Alas, I failed to save Sita, who charms like a child

But, I am content to die in Your service,” said that wise vulture from the wild

And looking intently at Rama’s face and into His lotus eyes

Jatayu closed his own eyes and gave up his mortal ties

A desolate Rama commanded: “May you reach the realm of permanence!

Ascend to ‘Vaikunta’ and decorate My space with your sacrifice and presence.”

 

Through this incident, Jatayu set an example for genuine devotion:

By sacrificing his life in the Lord's service, he derived satisfaction

Rama too demonstrated the duty the Supreme Being must discharge

Of giving salvation to all created beings, He alone was in charge

Though a bird, He blessed Jatayu with 'moksha', without any condition

He exposed His Supreme nature here, despite assuming a human incarnation.

 

Now Rama said: “The breaking of my heart should make a crack far greater:

How am I to handle my misfortunes that gape like a bottomless crater?

I lost not only my crown and my father, but also my beloved wife

All these I accepted—but, how to replace Jatayu, who forfeited his life?

Having lost my adoring father, I was happy to greet this bird magnificent

I wanted to live under his wings; but even that desire has turned out transient.”

 

Wailing thus, Rama lit a funeral pyre around Jatayu’s remains

And set it ablaze with His own hands, forgetting His pains 

Next, the brothers began moving in a southern direction:

To await further developments was now their only option

When night fell, Rama became inconsolable 

He sighed repeatedly at the loss of Sita, His wife adorable

 

"Oh, why doesn't the sun appear in the east and end my passion

Being my dynasty's head, he should show me some concern

Perhaps, he is ashamed of my inability to safeguard my wife

His name would be tarnished: over this, his mind must be in strife

But, unless he comes, the night and my sorrow will go on

And I shall languish this way and perish without seeing another dawn."

 

In this manner, Rama suffered acute torment

While Sita, on the other side, had all Her senses in a ferment

Rama became thirsty and Lakshmana went in search of waters

On his way, he was waylaid by ‘Ayomukhi,’ an ogress 

She lifted him up into the sky with a flourish

When Lakshmana failed to return, Rama fell into a deep anguish

 

Determined to take His own life, Rama prepared to light a fire

When He heard a sound as though someone were in straits dire

Fearing Lakshmana might be in danger, Rama turned pale

Towards the sound, at three times the speed of wind, He raced like a gale

Lakshmana had finished Ayomukhi off by the time Rama reached

And Rama beholding him thought, "A rule I have breached."

 

He said, "Lakshmana, we should always stay and move together 

Enough trouble have we faced by straying from each other."

Lakshmana saw Rama's distress and full of concern he said:

"I've killed the fiend who attacked me; Your anxiety, please do shed."

Soon came a day when the brothers felt surrounded by a vast force

It seemed to crush them in, allowing no escape or recourse.

 

Suddenly Rama began to laugh and said: “The demon army has us surrounded

We can vanquish them and rescue Sita before She is further hounded.”

But, the alert Lakshmana replied: “Out of your eagerness You see the demon troop

There are no battle indications—no war cries or drumbeats through the air swoop.”

Then they saw a monster’s arms and perceived a mouth in its belly

To imagine anyone could get away from its powerful clutch seemed too silly.

 

Rama cried: “So, this is how we are to end—oh, what a shame!

I am about to bring infamy to the Ikshvaku dynasty’s name.”

Lakshmana motivated Rama thus: “Don’t forget we are warriors

We must fight wholeheartedly and attempt to break down barriers.

My mother has asked me to give up my life in protecting Sita and You

I shall fight till my last breath; our fate You need not rue.”

 

With renewed vigour, Rama and Lakshmana hacked at the colossus

When they severed its arms, there arose a divine figure from the lifeless masses

This figure introduced himself as Kabandha, a denizen from heaven

And said “To take this form, by a sage’s curse, I was driven.

Rama, You are the beginning of and the reason behind all creation

Nevertheless, You stand apart from all material manifestation.”

 

In Your quest for Your consort, Rama, You need a friend’s support

From the monkey chief Sugriva, about Sita, You will get a report  

You must go further South and seek the ‘ashram’ of Shabari the ascetic 

She will give you directions to reach Rishyamukha and Sugriva, the rustic

Saying this, the being ascended to the regions where he belonged

Rama’s hopes revived, though his parting from Sita was prolonged.

 

Proceeding swiftly and eagerly, the duo reached Shabari’s abode

That disciple of Sage Matanga was awaiting them and bowed

After seating Rama comfortably, a variety of fruits Shabari proffered 

She bit and tested these for sweetness before they were offered

The gracious Rama, without dissent, accepted every part of her welcome

The elderly ascetic was by a sublime bliss overcome.

 

Why did Rama decline Guha’s food, but accept Shabari’s without discord 

Because Shabari served by her preceptor’s word, while Guha did of his own accord

The spiritual master’s true words become law even for the Supreme:

Hence, every being should follow an ‘Acharyan’ with spiritual knowledge extreme

Shortly, Rama asked for directions to the ‘Rishyamukha’ mountain

Giving the route in minute detail, many landmarks did Shabari mention.

 

Shabari then requested Rama’s permission: "I must drop this body, physical

Your loving gaze has neutralised my sins, inimical 

Let me go to Sri Vaikunta and involve in Your eternal service

Your lotus feet help anyone cross the materialistic precipice 

You shall meet Sugriva very soon and pledge friendship

With his aid, You shall find Sita and eradicate Her hardship."

 

After Shabari ascended to the highest realms, Rama and Lakshmana started

Once more on the quest for Sita, from whom they were now parted

They wanted to reach Lake Pampa, where lived Sugriva, the valiant ape

And with him forge a friendship that would their future mission shape

Sugriva lived there with his minister, Hanuman, and a band of loyal mates

He was in hiding from his unjust brother and biding his chance to turn the plates.

 

Why did Rama and Sita go through such difficulties?

They could have accomplished their mission using Their unlimited faculties.

But, the merciful Couple came down to earth to liberate Their children

To effectively free drowning mortals, They jumped into samsara's burning cauldron

While Rama had pledged to wipe out the diabolical forces

Sita voluntarily got imprisoned to liberate the divine women in Ravana's clutches.

 

When Rama entered the beautiful precincts of the Pampa lake,

His sense of despondency, the charming surroundings did rake 

He lamented, "O Lakshmana, this cheerful space intensifies Sita's absence

I do not know how to prolong life without her pleasing presence.

Look at how the fish that resemble Sita's eyes swim away and hide 

Though they try to prevent my tears thus, my eyes refuse to remain dried."

 

“The gods are injurious; they have stolen my jewel

On what my darling must suffer, I dare not dwell

Of Her alone, everything that is lovely reminds me

Till the day I see Her well, myself I shall not be

The nights seem extended and go on forever

The Sun, disdainful at my losing Sita, seems to rise never.”

 

Eventually though, the pleasing environs put heart into that woeful husband:

Rama became optimistic about rescuing His wife from Ravana, the brigand

The brothers quickened their pace and for Mt. Rishyamukha they headed 

With their upright and noble carriage, they resembled gods the evil dreaded

Standing beside the lake, the two surveyed the scenery

And recalling Shabari's pointers, further on, they began to hurry.
