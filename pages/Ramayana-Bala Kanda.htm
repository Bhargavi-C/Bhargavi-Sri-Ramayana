Bala Kanda

 

 

Prelude:

 

Dasaratha, the sinless emperor of Kosala, begets sons—

Rama, Lakshmana, Bharatha and Shatrughna from the heavens

The foursome grow up into strong, valorous and virtuous youth

Rama and Lakshmana fight their maiden battles to uphold Vedic truth 

The four brothers are married to Janaka’s daughters, comely

Rama destroys Parasurama’s pride by stringing his Bow of Vishnu firmly.

 

 

 

The Story in Detail:

 

For sixty thousand years ruled Dasaratha,

The kingdom of Kosala, with his capital at Ayodhya.

The mighty ruler had fought many a war and oft,

But the citizens knew nothing but his heart soft.

Even the heavenly denizens counted on his support;

He fought their demonic enemies as though it were a sport.

 

Kosala was a kingdom blessed with bounty and happiness 

Ayodhya was the city from where happened its business

Dasaratha considered himself only a caretaker of the kingdom

Accordingly his rule and every decree was backed by solid wisdom

The people had no needs: they were all fulfilled 

With neither theft nor beggary, their lives were goodness-filled

 

The King had but one burning worry

With no heir to protect his people, he felt sorry

Dasaratha's three chief queens—Kaushalya, Kaikeyi and Sumitra too

Wallowed and suffered for want of sons true.

Enquired the King of sage Vasishta, his preceptor,

A way to beget a son—a benevolent benefactor.

 

The sage realised the time was ripe—

The righteous' tears the Almighty must descend and wipe.

After much musing and careful consideration,

Suggested Vasishta with commiseration:

"Request sage 'Rshyashringa' to perform a 'Puthra Kameshti Yaga,'

For the world is ready to see the dawn of a new saga."

 

Dasaratha invited, with great humility and respect,

The sage with a bright and pleasing aspect.

Rshyashringa conducted the ceremony with reverence 

And it yielded the desired fruit in consequence

A divine form appeared from the Yaga Fire,

With a vessel of sweet porridge, not easy to acquire.

 

"Give this to your three queens" said the heavenly being

And Dasaratha took it with a devout and joyous bearing 

A half to Kaushalya and a quarter to Sumitra he presented

One half of a quarter to Kaikeyi he granted 

Left with the last one-eighth of the potion,

The King thought hard over the ambrosial portion.

 

The first half, Kaushalya swallowed, wishing for the heir,

Hoping for the Prince regent, Kaikeyi quickly had her share.

Sumitra, the ever wise and philosophical woman,

Calmly waited, wanting sons uncommon.

Dasaratha after due deliberation handed the remaining part 

To Sumitra, the queen after his heart.

 

Sumitra consumed the two parts given her,

Praying to the divine, two sons to confer.

Her first share would give a servitor par excellence 

To serve the eldest, the Lord Supreme, in every sense.

The ambrosial share would bestow a special son,

To serve the second, the Lord's devotee, with extreme devotion.

 

The heavenly denizens had prayed for divine intervention

As the wicked were torturing the virtuous without cessation.

The ever-merciful Lord Narayana to their request assented

And promised evil would be destroyed and good protected.

Earth had become heavy with the foes of piety

And to save it came down that original and foremost deity.

 

Ravana, the demon chief, through the severest penance

Had propitiated Lord Brahma and earned his benevolent glance

No one could destroy him, except man and monkey:

For his ultimate death, these exceptions proved the key

The Lord therefore decided to come down as man 

While the 'devas' to assist Him appeared in the monkey clan.

 

A remarkable statue of Sriman Narayana was with the Ikshvaku race

The kings in its lineage offered it prayers with devotion and grace

The Almighty however was not quite satisfied—

Even with thousands of years of worship, he was not gratified

So He chose the Ikshvaku clan for taking birth

To venerate Himself, to the possible extent of His worth!

 

Twelve months after the Puthra Kameshti, four sons were born,

Effulgent they looked, like the sun at dawn.

In the pleasant month of Chaithra, under the Punarvasu star on the ninth day

Came the first son, with eyes as cool and refreshing as the full moon's ray

On the next day, a son was born to Kaikeyi under the Pushya star,

While under the Ashlesha star, to Sumitra came twin sons without par.

 

After eleven days was fixed the time for the naming ceremony

The queens laid the babies in four cradles, rich and shiny.

Loud and troubled cries, the sons of Dasaratha emitted

Their persons, a puzzled Vasishta, to a close scrutiny submitted

"Oh, change the order in which the babies lie,"

He said—"They should then cease to cry."

 

"Put the first twin's cradle next to the eldest;

Next to the second son's, place the youngest."

Now, by half, the cries reduced

But by no means were they fully subdued.

Vasishta was thoroughly baffled

And wondered how the cries could be stifled.

 

Soon he told Kaushalya, the chief queen and proud mother

"Remove two cradles and lay the brothers together."

The instant it was done, the babies became quiet

Everyone realised: the slightest separation among the brothers meant disquiet.

Now the sage Vasishta had the pleasant duty

To name the princes as per their attributes and beauty.

 

The eldest looked virtually like a forest of lotus flowers:

His eyes resembled the petals of a lotus blossom that flutters

His nose matched a lotus' long and thin stem

His mouth, comparable to a lotus bud, was a veritable gem

His hands and feet were like soft lotuses in bloom

And His limbs and torso were lotus clumps that dispelled gloom.

 

"Rama" was the name given to the eldest son and heir

Gazing upon his 'rama' nay comely form, no one could tire.

"Bharatha" was the name bestowed on Kaikeyi's son

Who was destined to rule the kingdom as a ‘Bhara,’ a burden

Rama and Bharatha were dark of hue;

Telling them apart, very few could do.

 

Lakshmana and Shatrughna, the fair twins became

As divine attendants, they were to attain permanent  fame.

Lakshmana was the epitome of 'Lakshmi,' richness in service

Shatrughna considered direct service to God a 'shatru,' a disservice:

His sole duty was to serve the devotee of the Lord

In his lexicon, direct contact with the Lord was untoward.

 

Rama's incarnation was to establish obedience:

With what the elder says, the younger must show compliance.

Lakshmana's life was a standing example of 'seshatvam' or incessant service to God

Bharatha, even better, showed 'parathanthriyam': he served as per the wishes of the Lord

Shatrughna was the best: his character was difficult to get

On showing 'seshatvam' and 'parathanthriyam' to the Devotee, his mind was set.

 

Thus, Vasishta gave Ayodhya's princes names most apt,

While citizens in the kingdom looked on happy and rapt.

The sons of Dasaratha grew up fast

Under Vasishta’s tutelage their learning was vast

Before long, they reached twelve years of age 

And the King began to think about their marriage.

 

Just then Sage Vishwamitra entered Ayodhya city

Hearing of it, Dasaratha prepared to observe every nicety.

The sage accepting the king's hospitality, straight to the point came:

To grant a request and keep to it, was Dasaratha game?

Impulsive Dasaratha, well pleased, agreed most readily

He was used to making promises and sticking to them steadily.

 

Vishwamitra said: "Armies of monsters lay siege on my residence

They destroy divine sacrifices and of peace leave no semblance.

Send your eldest son Rama, the valorous one

To prevent my sacrifices from being undone.

He alone can annihilate every unscrupulous ogre

Tranquillity for the world, their obliteration would augur."

 

His words seared Dasaratha's ears like fire on wood

Dry went his mouth and still his heart stood.

"Oh sage, he is but a boy, not yet sixteen;

He is not even of age to enter a battle so keen.

Let me come instead, to render protection

I am amply qualified to perpetrate the demons' destruction.

 

At this response, Vishwamitra turned furious

And said, "So! Your promise is spurious!"

The world seemed to go red:

The sage's wrath caused a deep dread.

The wise Vasishta intervened in time and commanded:

"O King! You must do as the sage demanded."

 

"This sojourn with Vishwamitra is important

The world will know Rama as the Omnipotent."

To his preceptor's command, Dasaratha offered no protest

He yielded Rama up to Vishwamitra's trust

Everyone could see Rama comport Himself like a thing

That Dasaratha could hither and thither fling.

 

Vishwamitra, mollified, assuaged Dasaratha's fear

"You recognise Rama merely as your child, very dear.

But, I know that the entire cosmos is His child—

From everything mild to all that is wild.

You sit on a throne and indulge in worldly pleasure

I sit at the Supreme's feet and consider Him my treasure."

 

"You wear a crown, while I wear matted locks

I can recognise Rama, as one who forever to Him flocks.

You hold a sceptre, while I hold the 'Kusa' grass

I see Raghava as the Spirit beyond the material and crass.

Know thou, it's time for the Almighty to fulfil His purpose

Of destroying the wicked and defending the righteous."

 

Where man walks, his shadow follows—

Where Rama goes, Lakshmana goes:

So, with Vishwamitra went Rama and Lakshmana, the incomparable

As they followed the sage, their heroic bearing looked admirable.

Vishwamitra narrated many stories on the way

Thus, the long distances and time quickly passed away.

 

After leaving Ayodhya and upon reaching Sarayu's bank

Vishwamitra called the divine boys to him as the sun sank.

He had decided to impart 'Bala' and 'Atibala', mantras most powerful

And said: "Dears, utter them with care; they make you vigorous and watchful.

Though you have the said qualities in ample measure,

In keeping you free from thirst, hunger and sleep, the mantras would prove a treasure."

 

The princes, in all humility and deference,

Accepted the sage's lessons with reverence

Vishwamitra then instructed the youthful heroes to sleep

Many tasks lay ahead of them, demanding and deep.

Rama, ever eager for the Learned and Wise’s guidance

Was content to learn from Vishwamitra without hindrance.

 

The next day at dawn, Vishwamitra went up to the sleeping princes

He wanted to wake them in time for their morning chores and obeisances 

Looking upon Rama's sleeping form,

The sage got fully engrossed in His charm

Spontaneously, he blessed the mother who had yielded such a son—

Rama, who always gladdened minds, and hearts won.

 

Vishwamitra stood and stood, rooted to the spot:

Rama's loveliness is a net where every creature gets caught.

Finally, the sage shook himself and called with a soft diction:

"O! Tiger among men! Arise and get ready for action."

Rama and Lakshmana woke and concluded their morning duties

The trio then continued on their way, noting the surrounding beauties.

 

One day they reached a place exceedingly desolate

It was fit to make anyone forlorn and disconsolate.

Rama asked the sage for an explanation 

He replied: "It's Tataka, the demoness who destroys without discrimination."

Just then the demoness appeared on the scene

Her size, aspect, and demeanour—all were obscene

 

Vishwamitra ordered: "Quick Rama, kill her this instant."

But, Tataka's being a woman made Rama hesitant.

The sage urged: "She is a murderess, undeserving of pity.

Remember, killing her would relieve humanity."

Rama thought, “My father bid me obey the sage.

In pondering and vacillating, I must not engage."

 

The handsome warrior then shot a sharp arrow

It brought Tataka down like a wounded sparrow.

Flowers from the sky rained on Rama's head

The place sprang to life—all the barrenness fled.

Vishwamitra looked on contentedly:

He praised and blessed the boys unstintingly.

 

Highly satisfied, Vishwamitra spoke to Rama thus:

"Let me give you divine weapons with powers that impress."

The ‘Modaki’ mace and similar hand-held ‘astraa’ 

The ‘Narayana’ and ‘Pinaaka’ and other missile ‘shastraa’

The sage gave to Raghava with grave piety

And repeatedly blessed the illustrious one with safety.

 

"You have demonstrated not only courage

But maturity and wisdom beyond your age.

There is none better suited to use these weapons

In your hands they will become death to evil demons.

I gladly give them with my affection unfettered 

Use them well and in sheltering the good, remain undeterred."

 

The intelligent Rama accepted the missiles with concentration intense

He desired to get more missiles that He could use in defence.

Consenting, the sage bestowed annulment missiles on the Raghu scion

With all the acquired knowledge, Rama resembled a glorious lion.

The deities of the missiles clustered round the young warrior

And declared: "Serving you would ennoble our career."

 

The princes and Sage Vishwamitra then reached a locale lovely and tranquil

Eager enquiries Rama made and politely awaited Vishwamitra's will.

The sage informed: "This ascetic dwelling is called Siddha Ashrama

Originally, it belonged to the dwarf incarnation of Vishnu, Vamana.

This holy hermitage is a place of salvation

To those seeking the divine in a spirit of devotion."

 

Rama now inquired with concern and regard

When and where to mount their guard

"For six days and nights you must be vigilant

During which time the demons' attack is imminent

Our Vedic ritual, they will be raring to spoil

By dropping offensive objects, the very place they will attempt to soil."

 

 

Accepting the decree, the brave brothers took position

"No demon should defile this ritual," was their decision.

The relieved sages began their ritualistic activities

While the youth paraded, in anticipation of hostilities 

On the sixth night of the ceremony, the demons descended 

Towards wreaking havoc and perpetrating violence, their minds tended.

 

Mareecha and Subahu headed the vile fiends

They had no conception they were to meet their ends

Rama and Lakshmana were relentless in battle

And went through the demons like tigers among cattle

The evildoers froze and then scattered in confusion

In the youths, they saw death like an illusion.

 

Rama killed Subahu without any effort, apparent

And dispatched Mareecha miles afar in a moment

By these couple of acts, the compassionate Creator told

How He deals with the sins of those who come into His fold:

Past sins, with one hand, He exterminates 

Present and future sins, with another, He disintegrates.

 

The sages completed the ceremony in peace

Their vigil, Rama and Lakshmana could now cease.

Joy and harmony prevailed at the hermitage

The brothers having averted every mishap and outrage

Now, Vishwamitra and the other sages made a quick preparation:

Participating in a Vedic ritual at Mithila was their fond intention.

 

The sages addressed the gentle Rama in sweet tones

And suggested the brothers join them in their journey to the North zones

"At Mithila, ruled by the great-souled Janaka, you will find

A very special bow from the Gods, hard to bind."

Rama, gladdening at the prospect, agreed promptly

And soon they all sallied forth on the momentous trip jauntily.

 

Sri Mahalakshmi, Sriman Narayana's wife,

Took birth as Sita, Janaka's daughter and life.

The Supreme's chest is Her permanent residence

From that perch, She dispenses solace and benevolence.

The divine couple perpetually remain together

At the time of incarnations too, they invariably foregather.

 

For every soul, Sri is the trusty ally

Like a steadfast mother, she always stands by.

When Narayana is filled with ire on perceiving the sins prevalent

She wins His pardon with Her beauty and using fair argument

No one needs to worry with Her presence near

Neither his own sins nor the Lord's wrath, he needs fear.

 

Thus it is that every devotee desires Sri and Narayana's unity

For release from the birth-death cycle, their oneness provides the sole opportunity.

Rama and Sita's Union would bring universal welfare

It would become as unguent for those in despair.

The wise and virtuous world looked forward to the event

As one that would dispel darkness and make 'Dharma' prevalent.

 

At the time of Rama's visit, Sita had turned six

Her marriage to a suitable groom, Janaka wanted to fix. 

After intense thought, he had determined to hold a test 

To select from amongst Sita's suitors the bravest and the best: 

He thought, "The one to string the celestial Shiva's bow under my care, 

Would indubitably make divine Janaki an ideal pair."  

 

The monotony of that long and hard journey, Vishwamitra varied 

By regaling his companions with tales, he kept them unwearied 

Stories of Brahmadatta, and the river Ganga, and Uma and Karthikeya, her son 

Came forth from him in graphic detail and profusion 

Further came narrations about the milk-ocean's churning 

And Diti's seven sons becoming 'Maruts' or wind-gods with Indra's blessing.  

 

Vishwamitra did not omit to speak about his own ancestry 

Rama and Lakshmana listened with becoming attention to all that history. 

In due course, they all reached Vishala, ruled by King Sumati 

And spent a night there accepting his hospitality 

As they approached Mithila, they got a glimpse of its majestic skyline 

For that city, with the princes in tow, the sages made a beeline.  

 

On the outskirts, they came upon a serene dwelling 

Strangely though, the abode was devoid of any being 

Rama made enquiries of Vishwamitra inquisitively 

And the sage began to speak about the place knowledgeably: 

"This is where the great sage Gautama long ago resided 

Here, with his lovely and chaste wife Ahalya, he presided.  

 

Indra, the King of gods, had a wrongful desire 

Pleasure from Ahalya, he planned to acquire

Assuming the guise of Gautama, the shameless being came 

And sought Ahalya when she was alone, without fearing blame 

Though the exalted lady saw through the misguided god's disguise 

She accepted him, as her vanity rendered her momentarily unwise.  

 

Sage Gautama saw through what had occurred

And punished the duo for the sin they had Incurred 

Indra lost his testicles while Ahalya turned to stone 

The sage became Himalayas-bound, leaving his wife to atone. 

On Ahalya's pleading, the sympathetic Gautama soon amended his retribution: 

'Many years hence, from Rama's lotus feet, you shall receive purification'."  

 

Indeed, as the gentle Rama's feet touched the place 

Ahalya emerged from her curse, sanctified by His grace 

Sage Gautama too returned from where he had gone 

And reunited with his wife thereupon 

Together the couple worshipped Rama and made Him comfortable 

They thanked Him repeatedly, the two ascetics, formidable.

 

Seeing Rama's activities, Vishwamitra felt joyous and blessed

Fortunate was he to be getting Rama and Sita wed.

On, the group went and reached Mithila

The divine kingdom that housed the Goddess of fortune, Sita.

Janaka came forward, offering a grand welcome

With a joy inexplicable, he felt overcome.

 

Also appeared there Satananada, Janaka's priest and advisor

He knew, than Vishwamitra, there were few souls wiser.

While the elders exchanged civilities, the Royal brothers waited

Watching the eminent ones, they felt elated.

Janaka and Satananada expressed curiosity about the distinguished youth

The introductions were then made by Vishwamitra, the famous paragon of truth.

 

Satananada was grateful to Rama for restoring his mother

For, he was Ahalya's son and none other

The sage then narrated incidents from Vishwamitra's life as a tale

To the acute fascination and gratification of Rama, in vivid detail

Vishwamitra then desired Rama to "see" Shiva's bow:

The glorious lad was about to show His mettle, and how!

 

Five thousand strong men rolled in the cart holding the bow

Smilingly looked on by Rama, the One with the alabaster brow

For regular humans, the command "See" would mean just that, literally

But, for the high-souled Rama, "Lift and see" is what it conveyed subtly.

Accordingly, Rama went up to the cart and lifted the bow playfully

He strung it and pulled back the string nonchalantly.

 

A terrible sound rent the air at that instant

Causing the earth to reverberate like thunder distant

Most of those watching were stunned into swoons

Janaka was thrilled as if he had earned invaluable boons.

He spoke to Vishwamitra: "Gladly will I give my daughter in marriage

To this highly capable and potent young man of noble carriage."

 

Next, to confirm the alliance, he sent ministers to Rama's country

And to invite Dasaratha for the wedding festivities with kith, kin and gentry

The ministers did their bidding willingly and efficiently

And secured the nods of Rama's elders satisfactorily.

Anon, the people of Ayodhya, eager to see Rama's bride

Started for Mithila with delight pouring forth like a tide.

 

Arrived at Mithila, that capital of Videha, blessed by providence

Dasaratha got a respectful welcome and Janaka's warmth in abundance

Greatly content, the emperor of Kosala reciprocated

With gifts and amiable words, the good Janaka he venerated

The father's heart of Dasaratha welled up with pride

While Janaka's heart pranced and his eyes tears of joy, cried.

 

Rama and Lakshmana strode up to take blessings from their parents

Looking at them, Ayodhya's citizens felt affection and admiration in torrents

The elders, post discussion, decided to conduct the marriages of all four princes

Videha had four suitable brides whose beauty and character smote the senses.

Sita, sweet, wed Rama; Urmila, her sister, paired with Lakshmana,

Janaka's brother Kushadwaja's daughters, Mandavi and Shrutakirti married Bharatha and Shatrughna.

 

The wedding ceremony, per astrology and convenience, was fixed for an early date

Enchanting looked the brides and grooms—verily like gods incarnate.

When the 'Uttara Phalguni' star was on the ascend and the time auspicious

Rama and Sita were joined in matrimony, to the utter felicity of the pious.

The noble brothers of Rama too were married at the same time

To Sita's comely sisters, who well knew their duties prime.

 

Janaka, performing 'kanyadaan' said: "O Rama, here is Sita, my daughter

She'll accompany you as you tread the path of justice and virtuous matter

Take her with happy anticipation; May safety betide you;

Hold her hand in yours and become her all: this is your cue."

Upon which, Rama with joy and love boundless 

United with Sita, whose glories are fathomless.

 

King Janaka had to point Sita out explicitly—

For, Her form was visible everywhere to bewitched Rama, implicitly.

She came of the Videha dynasty, a clan highly reputable

And was totally worthy of Rama, the most estimable.

In chastity, goodness and values , she was steeped so strong

That she would never shy away from pointing out right from wrong

 

Such were the assurances from King Janaka

As he encouraged Rama to make haste and marry Sita

And just before urging Rama to clasp Sita's hand with His

Janaka imagined their joined palms and was lost to bliss

Recovering, he hurriedly blessed his magnificent son-in-law

Lest any 'evil-eye' should at the newly-weds claw!

 

Beholding the tiger-like Rama and the doe-like Sita together,

People experienced spiritual union with the universal mother and father.

It was as though time had come to a standstill at Mithila

And cocooned the world in a nameless felicity, away from illusory samsara:

The Super Soul is at the nucleus—like a peacock luxuriantly feathered

And every entity of the cosmos like a feather, to the Supreme is irrevocably tethered.

 

At the conclusion of the festivities, Dasaratha became desirous

Of returning to Ayodhya with the princes and princesses.

Janaka and Mithila's dwellers bade them a tearful farewell

And Kosala's entourage set forth after the exchange of rich gifts, without parallel

On the way, several birds set up an uproar frighteningly ominous

A worried Dasaratha asked his astrologers to explain those sounds, raucous.

 

The prescient men said: "For certain, there is trouble ahead;

But, it will vanish as mist under the sun—no need to dread."

Just then the sky turned dark and there fell a spooky calm

And Parasurama, the powerful ascetic with a violent disposition showed his form.

As he neared Dasaratha and his sons, He began addressing Rama directly:

"What glory did you gain by breaking an already damaged bow easily?"

 

Once the gods created between Vishnu and Shiva a rift

Shiva challenged Vishnu to a fight, his wisdom temporarily adrift:

The duo faced one another in an arena vast

And each made war-sounds more terrifying than a volcanic blast

Vishnu's 'humkaara' was enough to crack Shiva's bow—

It established His supremacy without further ado or show.

 

Parasurama was the son of Jamadagni and Sage Bhrigu's descendant.

King Karthaviryaarjuna became the cause for killing Jamadagni, the sage munificent.

Upon knowing this, 'Bhargava Rama' turned implacably wrathful

And vowed to wipe away twenty-one generations of Kshatriyas in full

He had mastery over weapons and a stock of powerful Arsenal

This ascetic turned revenge-seeker, who was not born to be banal. 

 

On seeing him, panic-stricken Dasaratha, made an earnest prayer:

"Spare my sons—they are mere boys, O great 'Kshatriya'-slayer!"

Parasurama, ignoring him, challenged Rama in a thunderous tone

"String 'Saarngaa' this bow of Vishnu I hold—then your potency I shall own.

If you thus prove you are at my level,

I shall then deem You fit for any duel."

 

Dasaratha Rama, unflinching and gracious, took the professed bow

And fit an arrow to it and gave Parasurama's aggressive arrogance a blow

Bhargava Rama then stood calm, having lost all his vigour and vanity:

Rama asked him for a target, for His poised arrows were endowed with irreversibility.

Parasurama offered as prey for the arrow the fruits of all his asceticism 

And before leaving to do penance, glorified Rama as the Superme, beyond criticism.

 

Having heard Parasurama's challenge, Dasaratha had started to tremble

He saw Rama only as his delicate child and in fear had begun to crumble

He had swooned before Rama could display His invincibility 

He awoke now to find everything in order and slowly regained sanity

Even the Gods appeared and praised the young Rama's incredible deed

Dasaratha's retinue then continued forward with renewed speed.

 

Upon their reaching Ayodhya, special celebrations broke out 

Spotting their beloved princes and their brides, the citizenry gave a jubilant shout

Rama and His brothers, with their respective wives

Enjoyed valuable family pleasures during that time in their lives

After a while, Bharatha and Shatrughna went to Kekaya for a stay

Rama was busy taking care of people's welfare, as was ever His way.
