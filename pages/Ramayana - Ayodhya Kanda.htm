Ayodhya Kanda

 

Prelude:

 

Dasaratha decides to declare Rama King 

Kaikeyi, on Kooni’s advice, obstacles does bring

Rama goes into exile, followed by His wife and Lakshmana

The parting from his son results in the death of Dasaratha

Bharatha, who was to become King in Rama’s stead,

Meets Rama and brings His sandals back to rule instead.

 

The Story in Detail:

 

Rama and the other princes spent twelve years in amity and conjugal bliss

In the perfect justice of Rama's conduct and service, none could find anything amiss

Dasaratha felt satisfied that his eldest son was fit to succeed as King

He called his counsel and made clear his longing:

"I wish to place my crown on Rama's young head.

Do I have everyone's consent and blessings to go ahead?"

 

Upon hearing this, all those gathered hailed him with a yell of glee

One bold youth even said, "O, old King, it's time for you to flee."

Dasaratha was thoroughly pleased with the welcome for his decision 

But felt rather taken aback and overwhelmed by the people's effusion.

He asked, eager to hear his son praised:

"How did Rama impress you? I wish to be apprised."

 

The ministers conveyed their opinion: "Rama is equanimous, just and merciful."

Vasishta and other learned men asserted: "To wise counsel He is ever open and respectful."

Vassals proclaimed, "Rama wears His qualities like so many crowns

He should wear Ayodhya's crown too: this we readily pronounce.

The common people declared, "Rama is mother and father to us

In fulfilling our needs and correcting our deeds, he is assiduous."

 

"Is that so?," exclaimed Dasaratha, his paternal heart full

And the citizens continued praising his son whose walk was that of a bull

"When there is a melancholy event, Rama mourns before the mothers rue

And when there is cause for joy, He celebrates before the men do

To Rama, we are always first and foremost;

We are His, and that is our grateful boast."

 

Dasaratha expanded with happiness and pride

"What good deeds did I perpetrate to beget this son?" he cried

In this manner, the whole of Ayodhya became immersed

In an ocean of happy contemplation, not knowing it was accursed:

For, difficult times were ahead of this great nation:

A direct result of the proposed coronation.

 

The King took Sage Vasishta's advice and decided

To place Rama on the throne the very next day, as guided

Dasaratha then sent for his son, and holding Him in a fond embrace said:

"Tomorrow, you shall be King with this empire under your head."

Rama, as was his wont, took the momentous news in a detached spirit:

Not succumbing to emotional upheavals was His especial merit.

 

Vasishta then instructed Rama to perform certain purificatory rites

And added: "Reflect on authority and its responsibilities; it will take you to heights."

A throng awaited Rama as He emerged from the meeting

They wanted to catch a glimpse of Him and offer their greeting

Rama headed for His quarters to prepare for the crowning

While the citizens, in untold merriment, slowly began drowning.

 

Manthara, Queen Kaikeyi's personal maid, saw the intense celebration

Curious, she enquired: "What's the cause for this jubilation?"

On being told about Rama's proposed ascension to the throne

The wretched hunchback let out a horrified moan

Rushing to Kaikeyi, she cried, "O dear, naive mistress!

Rama is to become king tomorrow: what more cause for distress?"

 

The delighted queen presented Manthara with her necklace

But the maid threw it aside in a frenzy, reckless.

"Rama shall be emperor and Bharatha His slave;

Kaushalya will be queen mother, while your position turns grave."

Thus, Manthara injected poison into Kaikeyi's simple mind

Till the queen completely forgot what it was to be kind.

 

Once Kaikeyi had saved the King from a battlefield

And by tending his wounds had got him healed

Dasaratha had granted her two wishes, feeling grateful 

Never suspecting they would drag him to his death, most dreadful.

Manthara now told Kaikeyi the means to achieve her ends

"Use the boons Dasaratha accorded you long back, without amends.

 

"Go and lie down in the 'Palace of Ire'

Remove all your jewels and lament till you hear Rama's sire

When he asks you to explain the reason for your animosity

Redeem the boons he had given you out of generosity: 

"Bharatha must become king in Rama's stead;

For fourteen years to the forest, He must go—the one Sita wed"

 

"Don't waver from your demands even a wee bit:

The King will try to dissuade you with emotional appeals and his keen wit."

Manthara's misleading words gave Kaikeyi a new resolution

She marched towards generating chaos and dissolution

Melancholy and misery, like dark clouds, loomed unseen

Very soon, there was to be a change of scene.

 

Kaikeyi resolutely walked into the palace where resentful queens went

The King should resolve their problems: for that, the place was meant

Dasaratha wanted to share the news of Rama's coronation with his favourite spouse

Learning where Kaikeyi was, he wondered, "What could be her grouse?"

Hastening to her presence and seeing everything helter-skelter

The poor King felt a cold hand upon his heart and went all a-twitter.

 

Dasaratha gently enquired of Kaikeyi what the matter was 

"I want to claim the two boons you gave," she said, after a pause

The relieved King cheerfully indicated his willingness

And Kaikeyi began to make her cruel requests with utter callousness

"Bharatha must be crowned King—that's the first boon I crave

For the second boon I ask for fourteen years of exile for Rama, the brave.

 

One moment her words hit Dasaratha's ear

And the next, he fell in a heap, struck with shock, sheer

Incredulously and with a pleading glance

He looked at Kaikeyi as though from an agonising trance

"Someone tell me I am in a nightmare

You cannot have uttered those words I am ill-attuned to bear."

 

But, Kaikeyi, with set face reaffirmed her demand

To the King, it became clear that she was beyond reprimand

Nevertheless, he tried reasoning with many appeals piteous

"O queen, when did you turn so unjustly covetous?"

When the King saw her unmoved, he attempted a compromise 

"I shall make Bharatha King—Rama would be pleased, it’s my confident surmise."

 

"But, please do not separate Rama from me

My very breath is at stake, I implore thee."

None of Dasaratha's pleas bore any fruit

He realised, his glory and happiness, Kaikeyi was about to loot

As an Ikshvaku scion, he could not go back on his undertaking

He had to bear the cruellest consequences in the making.

                  

"So be it—but, from this moment, you cease to be my wife 

Bharatha is no longer my son, remember this, you harbinger of strife,"

The King cried thus and fell in a faint

His hapless ordeal and suffering, no words could paint

Kaikeyi saw that she had to tell Rama the latest

She sent for Him, that Being who is noblest.

 

When Rama arrived and saw his father in a helpless state

He was worried and enquired of Kaikeyi how the king's agony could abate

Kaikeyi said, "Your father has decided to crown Bharatha instead of you;

To live in the forest for fourteen years is to be your due.

Do your duty and help your father keep his word of honour

That you are a devoted son, show now by your demeanour."

 

Rama, without receding from his calm even for an instant

Said, "O, dear father, over this decision, don't you lament!

Mother Kaikeyi, I shall do your bidding with alacrity

I look upon you on par with Kaushalya: I see no disparity."

Thus said Rama, with a smile that dazzled like the full moon

His face shone as if from a just-blossomed lotus it was hewn.

 

He prostrated at his father's feet and fell at Kaikeyi's too

And left to tell His mother what He was duty-bound to do

Kaushalya was shocked and dismayed by the king's action

"I shall go with you into exile, Rama," was her reaction

But her exalted son gave her sage advice on wifely duty:

"Stay and support your husband: make your station lofty."

 

Rama then went over to Sita to narrate the recent development

Sita got ready to accompany Her Lord during His banishment

Rama said: "O Sita, your delicate form cannot endure the hardship."

Sita replied: "I shall face worse pangs if I lose your companionship.

Oh dear husband, by recommending a varying rule

For Your mother and wife about wifely obligations, you will earn ridicule."

 

"My bag is all packed for a long sojourn in the wild:

I heard an astrologer predict the event, even as a child."

Janaki declared, "I shall go in front clearing every forest trail

Your soft feet shall walk the smooth paths: you need not ail."

Rama had to agree and soon proceeded to Lakshmana's dwelling

He beheld him standing there with emotions welling. 

 

"I am ready to leave, Rama," said that gem among brothers,

Who, for Rama's sake, any peril and suffering weathers

"If only you permit, I would rout the King and all his army

And proclaim you King: you know it's no big task for me."

The soft-spoken Rama calmed Lakshmana, nay Adisesha, the serpent:

"There's a divinity that shapes our ends," said He to that foremost servant.

 

"I want you to remain here and take care of Bharatha and our father

And be a pillar of support to Kaushalya, our dear mother."

Lakshmana laughed bitterly at Rama's suggestion

"I shall die from pangs of parting," he said with trepidation.

"Have you ever seen a fish survive out of water?

If you leave me behind, I shall not live a moment after."

 

Lakshmana then took leave of Sumitra, his intelligent mother 

She said, "This is the sole purpose of your birth—about anything else, don't bother."

"Support Rama at every step—do or die at His behest

Rama is your father and Sita your mother from this moment, dearest!"

"A word of caution, my child," continued Sumitra, that knowing soul

"Never look at Rama's walk, lest you should forget service, which is your goal."

 

"You may look at Rama while He sits or stands and even when He is asleep

But if you see His bewitching gait, your reason would be difficult to keep."

He walks in four distinct ways—angrily like a tiger and elegantly like an elephant

Majestically like a lion and proudly like a bull: make a note this instant!"

With this blessing and sweet advice ringing in his ears

The brave Lakshmana left to serve the duo for fourteen years.

 

Rama wore deer skin and matted His long hair,

Lakshmana too donned a similar garb, leaving his person bare

Sita, accustomed to dress in rich clothes and glittering ornaments

Cast them all away and wore plain and simple garments

The trio gifted away their belongings to Brahmins wise

And prepared to make their departure in a trice.

 

Two kinds of Dharma exist Sadharana Dharma and Visesha Dharma 

A righteous man is sometimes confronted with ‘Dharmic’ dilemma

He must consider and choose the one that serves the almighty better

And must let go of the other as mere material clutter

Sadly, Dasaratha forgot his 'Visesha' dharma:

He should have dropped his promise for Rama

 

Dasaratha failed to recognise his son as the highest truth

And so reached inferior heaven instead of the feet of that divine youth

But, wise Urmila, Lakshmana's wife, followed her Visesha dharma

She knew her husband's duty was to serve Rama

Accompanying Lakshmana on exile would prove a distraction:

Hence, the princess stayed behind without discussion 

 

Vasishta, the Royal guru, was appalled at the turn of events

Seeing the princess off on exile was his most unpalatable of moments

Sumantra, Dasaratha's charioteer, was charged to convey Rama

He found that wretched duty an unendurable trauma

Helping the unruffled Rama, Lakshmana and Sita into the Royal vehicle,

The loyal driver set forth on that fateful mission, inevitable.

 

Ayodhya's citizenry was profoundly shocked at the development so drastic 

Their frustration and anguish found vent in words caustic:

"The senile King has lost his reason—never mind!

We shall follow Rama wherever He goes; we shan't stay behind."

The men pursued their lofty prince, His dutiful wife, and their bodyguard:

And vowed to bring Rama back, even as they lamented hard.

 

Sita had never seen a forest, being delicately raised within royal grounds

"Is this our destination?" she asked as they came outside the palace bounds

Hearing this, people worried how She would face the jungle's dangers

But, to that princess sheltering under Rama's wings, hazards were mere strangers.

Through Her action—trusting completely to Rama—Sita showed:

People who surrender to the Supreme never feel cowed.

 

Sumantra drove the chariot fast at Rama's bidding

But the people, including aged Brahmins, pursued them unrelenting

On seeing this, Rama and His companions climbed down

And began walking towards the forest area far away from their town 

Presently, the entire group prepared to rest at the Tamasa river

The citizens continued to entreat Rama to return as their caregiver.

 

"Hey Rama, we feel impelled to follow you

We find happiness in Your company alone: You know our view

Towards You, even animals, birds and plants show utter devotion

About what they are suffering now, we cannot begin to mention

Either return with us to Ayodhya or desist from asking us to go back

For, Ayodhya devoid of You, is nothing but a desolate shack.

 

Talking in this vein to Rama, all those loving men

Fell asleep—determined to wake at dawn and follow Rama then

But, the object of their love woke much in advance

And for the welfare of His people, took a firm stance

Instructing Sumantra to create wheel marks leading into their kingdom

He left quietly with Sita and Lakshmana, that hero of martyrdom.

 

On waking the next morning and finding Rama gone,

That devoted crowd berated their sleep and turned forlorn

Following the wheel marks, they turned towards their home

Eventually losing the chariot tracks, they said, "We cannot forever roam.

Rama will keep His word to His father and will remain in exile

Let us live in Ayodhya, as He intends us to, though it be vile."

 

Traveling swiftly, Rama's troupe reached the borders of Ayodhya

They bid the sacred place farewell and reached the bank of the holy Ganga

Guha, the Nishadha King of the region, of prowess incredible

Received Rama with unconditional love, readily discernible

Guha said: "All that is mine is yours Rama, to command

Here I am, your servant, available always on demand."

 

Rama felt touched and pleased by Guha's reception

He called to Sita and said, "Bless him with your look of kind affection."

Turning to Guha, He said, "This Mythili, my wife, is your friend from today.

And your brother Lakshmana is my brother too, I say.

Till now, King Dasaratha had four sons—we were four brothers

With you, we have become five sons to our father and our dear mothers."

 

Thus did Rama show His generous nature and mingle with Guha

All beings were equal to that embodiment of 'Sousheelya.'

Rama acknowledged Guha's hospitality, but stuck to the exile's prescribed code:

He refused rich foods and into inhabited precincts, he never strode.

The exiled prince agreed to stay with Guha in the forest for one night

The hunter chief prepared beds of grass, ruminating sadly on Rama's plight.

 

While Rama and Sita slept on their designated beds

Lakshmana circumambulated them with slow treads

Guha, a tad suspicious about Lakshmana's intentions

Went behind that devoted brother, watching his actions

Guha's disciples, somewhat skeptical of their chief's motive

Dogged the footsteps of that irascible native.

 

The entire night passed in this strange manner,

While Rama and His consort rested peacefully in their corner

All the folks parading around and guarding them

Resembled planets circumambulating the sun at their realm.

Rama's impeccable form and character evoked such a reaction

It was ever so with everyone, there was no distinction.

 

The following morning, the hunter King pleaded with Rama to stay, 

But that handsome prince said: "After fourteen years, I shall return this way."

Next, He told Guha "We must cross the river."

Guha replied: "All three of you, across these waters, I shall carefully deliver."

Sita prayed to the river Ganga to carry them safely

"I shall thank you upon our return," She promised, solemnly.

 

Rama now implored Sumantra to return to the King

"Be a support to Father—to him, comfort you must bring."

The charioteer wanted to take messages from Sita and Lakshmana too

The bewildered princess could not utter any words, not even a few.

Lakshmana said: "Tell your King I no longer consider him my father.

Rama alone is everything to me: mother, father, master, and brother."

 

Following Sumantra's departure, Guha plied his boat with powerful strokes

The pristine surroundings charmed the simple-minded royal folks

After conveying the trio to the other side, Guha took his leave

He consoled himself: "My despair, the memory of Rama's kindness would relieve."

Rama, Sita and Lakshmana, though tired, were hopeful

Of finding a suitable place to stay and be cheerful.

 

Eventually, the illustrious three reached Sage Bharadwaja's hut:

That gratified elder welcomed and blessed Rama with eyes wet

"How did my King have the heart to make you wear deer skin;

More, how could he consent to see Sita in exile—Oh, what a sin?

Treat this poor dwelling as your own—it is my dearest desire

With You and Sita next me, I shall attain the salvation I most aspire.

 

Rama gently declined the offer citing Ayodhya's proximity:

"Frequent visitors would disturb this atmosphere of austerity.

Please suggest a place where my irreproachable wife would delight

And where we can follow the dictates of exile without a blight."

Bharadwaja recommended Chitrakoota as the ideal location

Where the three could remain isolated in peaceful contemplation.

 

That mountain of Chitrakoota turned out to be a pleasurable spot 

Filled with beautiful trees, streams, hills, cascades and what-not 

Edible fruits and roots too grew there in abundance—

Of physical and mental comfort, the place was a confluence 

Rama also spotted the hermitages of Valmiki and other ascetics 

And instantly chose to live among those eminent rustics 

 

Lakshmana quickly built a lodging upon Rama's instruction

They all entered their abode after offering due consecration

The hut built with timber and leaves proved charming

The three felt the agony of exile abate: cheer was in the offing

The river Malyavati, which flowed nearby, increased their pleasure

Watching the animals and birds in the region, made this time a treasure.

 

Sumantra returned empty-handed to Ayodhya to everyone's disappointment

He went straight to Dasaratha, there being no need for an appointment 

He found queens Kaushalya and Sumitra tending to the King, broken

Controlling his tears, Sumantra tried voicing words of comfort, as a token.

However, Dasaratha saw the charioteer's crestfallen face and understood 

"I knew He would not come back, my darling without falsehood."

 

Saying this, the loving father and unfortunate King fainted away 

The queens could only watch, their minds under agony's sway

When the King awoke, he narrated to Kaushalya an incident

"It happened when I was a youth and has lain in my mind, dormant.

My deep despondency has brought back that old memory

On being parted from my son, I should die: I earned that curse, hoary."

 

Dasaratha continued, "One day I went to the forest on a hunt:

Felling animals without directly looking at them was my special stunt

I heard a sound as of an animal drinking from a water hole

I shot my arrow, unaware it would take a horrendous toll

The next moment I heard an anguished yell

And realised I had committed a blunder enough to land me in hell."

 

"I ran in the direction of the sound, only to see a young ascetic writhing 

My arrow had pierced him fatally and he lay in pain, dying

Shocked, I ran to him and took his head upon my lap with fear

He whispered, "My blind parents, thirsty, await me not far from here

Give them water and confess your act—a thoughtless error

This is the only way to escape from a curse filled with terror."

 

"So I hurried sadly in the direction he had indicated 

And went near his old parents, to get myself vindicated

The mother and father, in a web of affliction, were caught:

They uttered a curse—it was with justice fraught!

Thus, on that fateful day, when I killed their son true

Separation from my son and consequent aching death was the punishment I drew."

 

“O Rama, my child. You like to be ahead of me always:

I wanted to renounce my stately duties and royal days

And take ‘Sanyas’ to live in the forest,

But, You went there first, my life, my dearest!

However, I do know how to get the better of you, my son

I shall overtake thee to the heavens and weep there for my pardon.”

 

All this Dasaratha mournfully spoke and fell insensible

To never wake and meet his death, terrible.

The two wives were struck with angst unendurable 

They had no sons near to console them in their trouble

Alas! Despite begetting the Lord Supreme as his son

Dasaratha's body lay embalmed: to perform his last rites, there was none! 

 

Inconceivable is fate that baffled even Vasishta, the sage

The options to handle the unfortunate situation, he began to gauge

He sent the swiftest messengers to the kingdom of Kekaya 

To bring Bharatha and Shatrughna back post-haste to Ayodhya

Refraining from sending any bad news, that dutiful elder

Hurried to make arrangements for the hereafter.

 

Bharatha and Shatrughna bid speedy farewells to their uncle

They feared some bad event had occurred in their circle

Hurrying towards their hometown, the two brothers encountered

Several bad omens that a catastrophe, portended

"Oh Shatrughna, my body trembles like a leaf in a storm

I fear something has transpired that is completely against the norm."

 

The princes rushed forward with all possible speed

And soon saw a neglected Ayodhya that made their hearts bleed

Bharatha galloped into his mother's palace and stopped short

Perceiving her widow's attire, he fell at her feet as if pinned by a dart

"Oh mother, have I lost my father, most affectionate?

How is my brother bearing up with this calamity sent by fate?"

 

At this, that stone-hearted Kaikeyi said: "Oh King, get up;

Your aged father has attained heaven; death is but a natural step."

On hearing this callous statement from his mother's lips

That pure-souled Bharatha went cold unto his fingertips:

"How can you be so casual and why do you call me King?

Tell me all; I am anxious—must I bear any further ill-tiding?"

 

"Your father sent Rama into exile for fourteen years

Sita and Lakshmana followed Him: O, wipe your tears.

I got the King to give me two boons—you shall have the crown,

While Rama remains in the forest; Oh, do remove that frown."

Bharatha at this wailed loudly and smote his head on the ground:

"How am I alive still? My life-force must be very sound!"

 

"You female monster! What made you think evil for my Rama?

So, it was you who killed my father, unable to control your Kama?

How did you believe I would fall in with your unjust wishes?

I shall now proceed to kill you, you serpent with tresses!

I declare, you are no longer my mother

I fail to recognise you, you are a thorough stranger."

 

Now Shatrughna hurriedly restrained Bharatha with a caution:

"You will wound righteous Rama—control your passion."

Bharatha, collecting himself, ran towards Kaushalya's residence

He wanted to fall at her feet and mourn their providence

Upon sighting Bharatha, the wilting Kaushalya asked:

"With seeking further boons, have you been tasked?"

 

"Oh mother, your words have made my spirit perish

Do you believe all this happened in accordance with my wish?

If that were true, let every kind of sin fall to my portion

And let me go to netherworlds from where there is no redemption."

Saying this, the gentle-hearted Bharatha slid to the floor

And Kaushalya, realising her mistake, was angry no more.

 

"Dear Bharatha, that I should think you capable of harm

Shows I am not myself: I have lost my customary calm.

In my grief at losing my husband and son simultaneously

I have wounded you to your heart, grievously

Oh dear son, you are the greatest King Ayodhya has seen yet

You shall make a fine ruler—l see why you are Rama's pet."

 

Bharatha said, "Mother, don’t address me as King—that I shall never be.

Rama shall be ruler and I, His eternal slave: I promise thee.

I shall seek Rama this instant and bring Him here

He shall decorate the throne while I serve Him: don’t fear."

On hearing these sweet words, Kaushalya became delighted

She repented once more: “Your untainted mind I shouldn’t have slighted.”

 

Now Bharatha went to meet their guru and well-wisher

A relieved Vasishta said, “There isn’t a moment of leisure.

The king’s body awaits its religious last rites

Shatrughna has to perform them: Dasaratha nullified your rights.”

Bharatha laughed at the irony: “When I am no longer one of the heirs

Why leave in my hands Ayodhya’s affairs?”

 

But, the dead’s last wishes had to be respected:

So, Shatrughna cremated his father’s body as expected

Bharatha told Vasishta: “I cannot accept the crown

It’s a sin and will fail to bring me renown

We must go and meet my brother, dear and just.

We will bring Him back and these anomalies, adjust.

 

Vasishta was pleased and said: "Yours is a fair proposal 

But, understand! Ayodhya's security is not at my disposal.

The kingdom needs a King without further delay

Accept the crown before convincing Rama, this is no time to parlay.”

To this Bharatha replied, “O revered one, why do you steal

My only treasure—the servitude towards Rama, which I feel?”

 

The guru argued, “I know you are Rama’s property

But, one property sometimes guards another for security:

Does not a treasure-chest protect a valuable gemstone?

In the same spirit, why can’t you sit on Ayodhya’s throne?”

Intelligent Bharatha rejected the simile saying, “Do ponder—it is the owner

Who puts one property in charge of another.”

 

“Rama’s orders alone, I always await

That is the fundamental mark of my trait

I shall depart now in search of Rama, the avatar of truth

Any other action, I reject as unworthy and uncouth.”

Upon hearing Bharatha’s resolute decision

The citizens shouted approval and joined his mission.

 

Sumantra led Bharatha, melancholic and pensive

While the others followed optimistic, yet restive

Soon the party reached the banks of Ganga’s holy waters

Where Guha had met Rama and secured his release from material fetters

From far away, Rama’s friend spotted the large army

Suspecting Bharatha of wrong aims, he became prey to emotions stormy

 

“Get ready, my followers: here is battle at our doors

Anyone who thinks of harming Rama must first subdue our force

Rama has honoured me by declaring me His friend

If I hesitate to put down my life for Him, I shall be a fiend.

Let Bharatha bring an army that resembles a sea

He cannot intimidate us: that, he will soon see.”

 

Meanwhile Bharatha, having reached the river

Silently stood by, his limbs and body in a quaver

Sumantra had the sensitive task of making the introductions

He could perceive Bharatha and Guha’s passionate demonstrations:

While Bharatha was in a state of insensibility

Guha was wrathful and bubbling with volatility

 

But, as Guha came closer and clearly saw Bharatha’s person

He wondered: “Resembling Rama so closely, can he think of treason?

This prince, bathed in tears, can never have thoughts nasty

In suspecting him, I have been unintelligent and hasty.”

Sumantra now began to introduce Bharatha to the hunter chief:

“O Guha, behold Rama’s younger brother Bharatha, who stands swathed in grief.”

 

Next, the faithful charioteer turned to Bharatha and said

“Here stands Guha, Rama’s friend, and the hunter community’s head.”

Upon which, Bharatha greeted Guha with folded hands and asked:

“Please tell me how Rama spent His time here and how in His love, you basked.”

“Rama spent a night here,” said Guha, “and upon us, benevolently He gazed

Lakshmana served Him incessantly and throughout, remained unfazed.”

 

Bharatha expressed pleasure at Lakshmana’s love and care

He eagerly asked, “In this rough forest, how did Rama and the others fare?”

Guha then showed the corner where Rama and Sita had slept

“O cruel Kaikeyi, look at what you did!” Bharatha bitterly wept

And in uncontrollable emotion, he collapsed at Guha’s feet

“How did dainty Rama sleep on this coarse grass—indeed it’s a rare feat!”

  

Later, Guha helped Bharatha and his retinue cross the holy waters

They all came upon Bharadwaja’s ashram where anxiety scatters

“O Bharatha, do you want to harm Rama?” asked the sage bluntly

The unjust words froze that loyal brother—he gaped silently

“Even the omniscient sage suspects my intent,” he sobbed

“Of all my dignity and character, today, I stand robbed.”

 

Bharadwaja immediately amended his words and said in a soothing voice:

“I wanted to show the world your greatness—don’t lose your poise.”

Mollified, Bharatha told the sage what he had in mind

On learning which, remorseful Bharadwaja, with tears became blind.

He said: “You stand alone, my dear child of destiny

Only the thoughtless would put your love for Rama under scrutiny.”

 

The sage then pointed out the directions to reach Chitrakoota

That now ensconced Rama, Lakshmana and Sita.

Eager to reach his brother, Bharatha set the course swiftly 

And with every step that took him closer, he became tearful repeatedly

Meanwhile, Lakshmana, keeping a lookout for danger

Spotted Kosala’s flags and a huge army approaching from yonder

 

Darting into the hut he had built, Lakshmana dressed himself for war

Astonished, Rama asked: “Have you sighted any peril afar?”

“I see Bharatha accompanied by a large army 

He moves in our direction—his wicked intent is clear to me.

Here I am, an army unto myself, to screen you

Let any number approach—they shall get their due.”

 

With a pacific smile, Rama said: “You are making a mistake.

Don’t you understand Bharatha is here for my sake?

If, as you say, he has come resolved to fight me,

That thought alone is my death—this, I tell thee.

I know my brother like the back of my hand

Put away the weapons; come next me and calmly stand.”

 

Bharatha, seeing Rama from afar, stumbled forward in a rush

As he dropped down at Rama’s feet, the entire place fell into a hush

Bharatha’s supreme devotion permeated the surrounding

Many minutes passed before anyone there could begin talking

Rama said to Lakshmana with a meaning glance:

“Observe My brother Bharatha and his unswerving stance.” 

 

“If you are interested in ruling our kingdom, I shall make the arrangement:

Bharatha will gladly stay with me—he only awaits my statement.”

Rama never brooked insult to His devotees from any quarter

Hence, from being stern with Lakshmana, He did not falter 

Bharatha now imparted the news of their father’s death

And Rama congealed into a statue, devoid of life’s breath

 

Struck with terrible grief, Rama began wailing presently

“Your father’s gone; your in-law is no more” he cried loudly

Even in that moment of deep anguish, Rama would not accept

His father had perished: He saw Lakshmana in Dasaratha’s place in every respect:

When someone assents to hold Rama as his or her all-in-all

Rama made that soul His everything, once and for all.

 

After completing his father’s last rites, Rama enquired about Bharatha’s rule

“Are you being fair and compassionate, as we learnt in Vasishta’s school?

Are your decrees in accordance with what ‘Dharmic’ principles bid?

Are your actions in alignment with what our ancestors did?

To this Bharatha replied, “All your earlier questions are irrelevant

Only the last question is valid: towards tradition, I am ever reverent.”

 

“You and King Dasaratha changed the custom of kingship

I shall set it right—you alone being fit for that worship

You know I am Your servant and nothing more

I haven’t become King—that would’ve wrecked my very core

Please accept the kingdom and its responsibilities

Return with us and honour our sensibilities.”

 

Rama attempted to argue Bharatha out of his decision

But failed to sway him from his conviction

And though everyone pleaded, Rama too remained adamant

“I must keep my promise and ensure truth’s establishment.”

Nevertheless, Rama landed on a compromise arrangement

“Take my sandals; they shall rule on my behalf, as my replacement.”

 

A person pawns a valuable artefact:

He gets a fraction of its worth for his act

Similarly, Rama pledged with Bharatha His priceless footwear

And extricated Himself from Ayodhya, as it were

Thus, more than Rama, His ‘Paduka’ are valuable: 

In sheltering His devotees, They are more capable.

 

Rama's 'Paduka' or sandals were originally very proud

They felt: “Of exquisite qualities, our owner possesses a whole crowd.” 

When they saw Bharatha, begging Rama to return, with a devout heart 

They wondered, "This noble soul's wishes, would Rama dare thwart?

Rama has two paths opening out in front of him

Either keeping His promise or relenting to Bharatha's appeals—choices grim. 

 

Is Rama going to follow 'Sadharana dharma' and obey His father? 

Or choose His 'Visesha dharma' and go back to His brother?"

When Rama chose 'Sadharana dharma' His sandals decided:

"No longer shall we stay here; our pride has subsided 

Rama should have fulfilled His devotee's pleas

Even if it meant His own honour not remaining in one piece." 

 

Thus, the 'Rama Mani Paduka' took Bharatha away and adorned his head 

And ruled justly over Kosala in Rama's stead

When Bharatha left Rama's presence, he had a smile on his face 

But, alighting at Ayodhya, of gloom alone he showed trace.

On the one hand, he was happy: he had discarded kingship

On the other, he was sad: having temporarily lost Rama's companionship. 

 

Bharatha installed the 'Paduka' on Ayodhya's royal throne

Wearing ascetic garb and undertaking austerities, he shone

He had pledged to Rama: "For fourteen years, I shall count each day and not tire 

But, if I don't see you on the morning after, I shall jump into fire."

Staying at Nandigrama, on Ayodhya's border

Bharatha ruled well and in Rama’s kingdom, kept order. 

 

A while after Bharatha left, Rama noticed anxiety among the sages 

Who lived in the vicinity of His lodging in various hermitages 

Upon enquiry, that merciful annihilator of demons discovered 

That many ascetics had been attacked and murdered 

Also that the demons, enraged by Rama’s presence

Had been harassing the sages and wreaking their vengeance 

 

When the savants requested leave to quit that place, Rama agreed 

He too chose to leave Chitrakoota, as those wise people had decreed 

On the way, Rama visited Sage Atri and offered His salutations

Well content, that venerable personality bestowed his benedictions

The elder then asked Sita to meet his aged wife, Anasuya 

Who was so exalted that none could envy her, that is, feel ‘asuya’ 

 

That astute lady instructed Sita on wifely obligations and conduct

And explained how the wrongs of a husband a wife must endeavour to correct 

She then said in passing: “But, since Rama’s divine attributes always dance 

To display ‘pathi vratha dharma’ you won’t really get a chance!”

At this Sita, stung, said: “Two types of love and respect exist

‘Guna Krita Dasyam’ and ‘Swaroopa Krita Dasyam’, as the wise insist.” 

 

“When people love others for their attributes, they demonstrate the former 

When someone loves another for their sake alone, they showcase the latter.

My devotion for Rama emanates from our relationship and stands for His person

Hence, whatever He does, I shall love Him the same: this I must mention.” 

Anasuya, well satisfied with this reply, asked Sita to describe her nuptials 

“Please tell me all about how you wed Rama, who surpasses celestials.” 

 

Sita began to narrate Rama’s entry into Mithila, with Lakshmana in tow

And His incredible and effortless breaking of the ‘swayamvara’ bow

She then described the various highlights of the wedding ceremony

And told of the celebrations, gifts, and donations that were many

Anasuya, thrilled with Sita’s meticulous explanations, gifted her many a jewel 

She then fondly declared: “Wherever you live, harmony shall dwell.” 

 

Rama, Sita and Lakshmana soon took leave of those aged ones

And walked deeper into the forest to locate a new abode, per their directions 

Rama in the front, followed by Sita, and Lakshmana

Gave a life-like impersonation of ‘Aum’, the ‘Pranava’:

The Paramatma is at the fore; the Jivatma takes the rear

Sri bridges the two by her presence, sheer.
