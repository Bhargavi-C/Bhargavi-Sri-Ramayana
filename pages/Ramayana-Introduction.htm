SRI’S RAMA 

(Sri Ramayana on the lines of the Srivaishnava Sampradaya)

 

 

 

Mangalam

 

Sri Rama, my dear Lord,

Your glory sang Valmiki, the divine bard.

My salutations at his feet—for his merciful gift

and at Your lotus feet—for becoming that very gift.

May the Ramayana live for eons and more,

And show the way to you—the Core.

 

Sri Ramayana—the story of Dharma, nay, Sri Rama,

Shows the path of surrender to every ‘Jivatma.’

With Sita, Lakshmana, Hanuman, and Vibhishana, 

And with the loving Bharatha and Shatrughna,

In these holy ‘shlokas,’ the at once simple and great ‘Purushottama’

Stands revealed as the peerless ‘Paramatma.’

 

 

----------------------

 

Sage Narada and Sage Valmiki's Discussion 

 

 

One day at Sage Valmiki’s ashram, there arrived a glorious visitor

The sage was happy to welcome the universal preceptor:

The ‘Muni pungava’ Narada, the one who dispels ignorance and grants wisdom,

Had come to bless Valmiki with the ‘Sankshepa Ramayanam’

Valmiki was well-pleased and fell at Narada’s feet,

He played the perfect host and offered the muni an exalted seat.

 

Sages always sought knowledge about the Supreme,

so as to guide all life into the ultimate stream

Valmiki too was cast in the same mould,

And the highest truth, he waited to be told.

For, Narada was a devotee par excellence,

A repertoire of wisdom who could impart true sense.

 

“Who is the ‘Gunavaan’, the One with the famed sixteen virtues?

Is there someone, on this earth, at this time whom you can choose?”

Thus asked Valmiki of Narada, with bent back and covered mouth,

And waited with bated breath for the precious answer to pour forth.

Narada’s eyes welled up and tears flowed fast

till he could neither speak nor stand, ruminating on the Resort last.

 

“Let me recollect,” Narada said in a choked voice:

But, not because he had to make a choice.

He knew Rama to fit Valmiki’s description and beyond,

And bringing His form to his mind’s eye, he was overcome by thoughts fond:

Continued the radiant sage: “Give me a moment, O child,

my joy is running boundless and wild.”

 

Valmiki, meanwhile, was amazed at what he saw:

He had always thought sages are ‘Gyanis’ who neither cry nor thaw.

Devotion and surrender were new to him,

He was yet to transcend knowledge's rim.

Only then did he learn that knowledge ripens into devotion,

and that exalted tears are its beginning and conclusion.

 

Narada’s face became suffused

with a delight pure, as he mused.

He then spoke: “Yes indeed, there is One,

Rama is His name; He comes of the Ikshvaku dynasty and is Dasaratha’s son.

He has superior qualities that are too numerous to count,

And has not a single bad quality he must surmount.

 

He is the embodiment of 'Sousheelya,'

The quality of mingling with all without high-low criteria."

Went on Narada with enthusiasm, "Let me recount the story of Rama;

That has it all—Dharma, Artha, Kama, and Moksha."

The précis of the Ramayana Narada now told

It became the prelude to the story Valmiki was to unfold.

Sankshepa Ramayana (The Précis of the Ramayana)

 

It was a period when virtues had begun to dwindle—the ‘Thretha Yuga’ 

The era that had dawned after more than seventeen lakh years of ‘Satya Yuga’

‘Sriman Narayana,’ the Absolute Divinity, had incarnated twice in the ‘Thretha’ age

As ‘Vamana’ and ‘Parasurama’ to conquer the evil causing ‘Dharmic’ damage  

Eventually, the time came for the Lord to appear again—as Sri Rama, the Divine Man 

And events began to transpire to make way for His birth in the Ikshvaku clan

 

As the crowning jewel of the Ikshvakus did Rama appear

Possessing immaculate qualities, he stood without a peer.

His appearance, valour, manner, and character—

Everything about Rama was exquisite like nectar.

His knowledge of the scriptures and skills in archery

Were too great to admit rivalry.

 

Dasaratha, an eminent scion of the Raghu clan

Ruled Ayodhya for a sixty-thousand year span 

To that distinguished king were born four illustrious sons

Rama, Lakshmana, Bharatha and Shatrughna who shone like suns

The four became experts in warfare and other princely pursuits

To their loving and humble qualities, there were no substitutes 

 

Emperor Dasaratha decided to crown Rama future King

And set the coronation for an auspicious day in spring.

But Kaikeyi, his queen, claimed two boons given her long ago:

Bharatha must be King and Rama into fourteen years of exile, must go

The hapless King, bound by his word,

Banished 'Raghava' to the forest and fell like a wounded bird.

 

While Rama got ready to fulfil His father's command;

To accompany Him was His brother Lakshmana's only demand.

Rama's doting wife Sita too decided to follow Her consort:

Her firm resolution, none could distort.

Ayodhya's citizens also went with the three virtuous ones

They could ill-bear separation from their Princess and Dasaratha's sons.

 

Swiftly sped Rama in a chariot driven by Sumantra,

Stopping at Shringiberapura with the tribal chief Guha.

Rama then sent Sumantra back with a soothing missive to His father,

And to preclude constant visitors, He went on farther.

On Sage Bharadwaja's advice, He moved to the Chitrakoota forest,

And there lived happily with His wife and brother, dearest.

 

Sumantra returned and conveyed Rama's resolution 

Upon hearing it, Dasaratha gave up his life in piteous lamentation.

The noble Bharatha stood steadfast in refusing the kingdom

He only desired a place under Rama's Feet, being the epitome of wisdom.

He sought Rama and begged Him to accept Ayodhya's throne

But, Rama refused, wanting to complete the exile as He had sworn.

 

Seeing Bharatha stubborn and reading his mind,

Rama bestowed His sandals on his brother, being kind.

Said He: "Till I return, let these 'Paduka' rule on my behalf."

Bharatha left, reluctant but staunch, crying like a bereft calf.

He lived away from Ayodhya at Nandigrama, a village

And awaited Rama's return, dressing and living like a sage.

 

Meanwhile, Rama went even farther, on to the Dandaka forest;

He thought, “Residing far from My kingdom is best.”

Soon, Rama killed the cursed Viradha and visited many sages

He learned about the demons torturing them for ages.

Sage Agastya bestowed on Rama weapons capable of wiping sorrows—

Namely, Indra's bow, sword, and two ever-replenishing quivers of arrows.

 

Rama took a noble vow, to eliminate the cruel demon race

And awaited an opportunity to bring them down in disgrace

He disfigured Shurpanaka, the guise-changing demoness,

Among her enormous army Rama scattered death like largesse

Shurpanaka's vindictive brother Ravana, wanted to harm Him,

And sought help from Mareecha, who changed forms at whim.

 

While Mareecha distracted the valiant brothers, Rama and Lakshmana,

Ravana abducted Sita, being an evil demon given to "Kama."

Jatayu, the vulture king, fought Ravana, with great pluck 

And informed Rama of Sita's abduction, despite being fatally struck. 

Rama lamented His losses, but soon recollected Himself;

He vowed to rescue Sita, indistinguishable from His self.

 

Searching for Sita, Rama and Lakshmana wandered far and wide

And saw Kabandha, engaged in plunder and covered in gruesome hide

When Rama killed and cremated him, he rose heavenward and directed: 

"You must meet Shabari, an old ascetic woman, most blessed."

Rama visited Shabari and accepted her loving hospitality

At Lake Pampa, He met Hanuman, the monkey with enormous vitality.

 

Hanuman took the resplendent brothers to Sugriva, his chief,

His story, ending with Sita's abduction, Rama then narrated in brief

Presently Rama and Sugriva swore alliance before a fire:

And forged a friendship of which they would never tire

Sugriva explained in detail the feud with his brother Vali

Loyal Rama promised to punish the monkey King for his cruel folly

 

Sugriva then challenged Vali to combat

Vali raged: "Where from gets his new courage, the rat?"

Tara, his clever wife, warned him of Rama's invincible power

But, Vali, failing to heed her, rushed out: alas, to return never.

The monkey brothers then clashed fiercely

Rama's arrow pierced Vali and took his life deftly.

 

Rama established Sugriva as the King of Kishkinda

Next, Sugriva helped locate the princess of Mithila

Monkey armies he sent in every possible direction,

They were to search for mother Sita, that was the sole condition.

Sampati, the elder brother of Jatayu, pointed to Sita at Lanka

Hanuman leaped across the intervening ocean, fully a hundred Yojana.

 

Soon Hanuman spotted Sita in Ravana's Asoka garden

A personification of chastity, patience and pardon.

Hanuman slowly went up to her and presented Rama's ring;

And when he described Rama, Sita rejoiced as though it were spring.

Then Hanuman began wrecking trees, wanting to gauge Ravana's strength

To fight and quell him came demon after demon, at length.

 

Hanuman rampaged and routed several powerful demon captains

And pretended to yield to Indrajit's weapon, though capable of smashing mountains

The son of Ravana took the monkey to his father's presence

And there Hanuman, with the demon king, held an audience

He then burnt Lanka with his tail, which the demons had set ablaze

But ensured Sita remained safe while Lanka's citizens went into a daze

 

The venerable ‘vanara’ then returned to Rama, all jubilant,

"Seen Sita," he announced being sensitive and intelligent.

Rama, with Sugriva and the other monkeys reached the ocean shore

Vibhishana, Ravana's virtuous brother, sought succour at Rama's door.

Rama next prayed to the ocean-god, and when he failed to appear,

The enraged Lord swore to make him forever disappear.

 

The ocean-god relenting, sought pardon and shelter

And suggested Rama build a bridge over his calmed water

The monkeys laboured eagerly in the Lord's service

The opportunity to serve Rama, no one wanted to miss.

Raghuveera, the brave lion, vanquished Ravana in battle,

Though victorious, something yet remained for Him to settle.

 

That Sita must prove Her spotlessness, Rama did indicate

She entered fire instantly: to Her Husband She was ever ready to supplicate 

The fire-god couldn't harm Her, the embodiment of chastity,

Rama took Her back gladly, having established Sita’s purity

Rama then placed Vibhishana on Lanka's throne and pronounced him King

With God-given boons, the dead monkeys to life did He then bring.

 

Rama stepped into the ‘Pushpaka,’ the divine aircraft,

Towards Ayodhya, He ordered it to quickly waft.

En route, He visited Bharadwaja, the omniscient sage

And sent Hanuman ahead to meet Bharatha with a pleasing message

Rama followed later in the ‘Pushpaka Vimana’ with His supporters, faithful

To be reunited with His brothers, firm and ever truthful.

 

Soon, Rama removed His matted locks and also His hermit raiment

His brothers followed suit, to the citizens' untold joy and merriment.

Then the valiant Raghuveera and Sita attired themselves in regal splendour

And ascended the throne majestically in glory and grandeur.

Then predicted Narada, the sage who could, time and space, pierce:

"To the ‘Parabrahma Loka’ Rama would journey, after ruling for eleven thousand years."

 

He also said: "Rama Rajya will remain an ideal to emulate

For eons to come, people's admiration it will stimulate.

The holy Ramayana eradicates sins

Being in conformance with the Veda, wise hearts it wins.

Anyone who engages with this story of Rama, as it happened,

In the highest echelons of this world and the next, would be positioned."

Valmiki Receives a Boon from Lord Brahma

 

Soon after Narada's benevolent visit, Valmiki one day saw

A hunter striking down a bird, a ‘krauncha.’

The dead bird's poor mate let out agonised cries;

They stirred sage Valmiki deeply, though he was devoid of ties.

Valmiki called loudly for destruction upon the hunter's head,

And walked away with a swift and furious tread.

 

The sage turned unhappy with his outburst

Though the curse had been an elegant verse and his very first.

Because, instead of being a blessing auspicious, 

It had turned out a malediction, most pugnacious.

Valmiki thought: "Its grammar, metre and words are perfect,

Yet, it's not something the wise would respect."

 

The sage returned to his 'ashram' and sat brooding over his haste

Presently Lord Brahma appeared and said: “Your verse shall not go to waste.”

He then rearranged Valmiki's words—now they extolled Rama as the best—

and bade the Sage compose The Ramayana with zest * 

Thus did the seed get sown 

for the towering “ithihasa” of renown.

 

Brahma next bestowed a boon—"You can comprehend through your mind's vision

Everything Rama thought, said, and did, without omission.

The Ramayana would live as long as Dharma prevails

People would turn to it as an answer to all travails.

You will render the Ramayana as a service to mankind, oh Valmiki,

Your fame shall continue to live for one and all to see."

---------------------------

Valmiki begins the great work 

 

With Narada, the preceptor's blessing and Brahma's boon,

The Sri Ramayana, Valmiki got ready to compose and tune.

He closed his eyes and controlled his faculties,

And instantly began to experience Rama without any difficulties.

What he saw and heard and felt, the sage wrote down in song,

And became steeped in divinity and devotion before long.

 

As he lived with Rama, the all-pervading super-soul

Valmiki transcended the (im)material and catapulted towards his goal.

From him dripped words of nectar with esoteric meaning,

Vying to talk about Surrender—the culmination of all learning.

The greatness of Rama, Sita or Their devotees—who can decide

Which of these, in the Ramayana, do reside?

 

The Ramayana showcases many ‘Surrenders’ through its length

Some of these bore fruit, while others lacked that strength

The demigods' surrender to Sriman Narayana for saving the pious succeeded

But, Dasaratha's surrender to Parasurama for sparing Rama proved deluded

Rama accepted Lakshmana's surrender and took him on His exile

But, He chose to decline Bharatha's tearful surrender to overrule Kaikeyi's guile

 

Vibhishana's surrender, the central point of the Ramayana,

Demonstrates in full the components of the 'Sharanagati tatva':

“Do what the Lord likes and refrain from what He does not

Place resolute faith in Him and pray for His merciful onslaught

Tell Him there is nothing to offer and place the soul at His feet”

By doing these six, one completes the best possible feat.

 

Rama's surrender, the final one, to the ocean King failed miserably:

What answered to the others couldn't relieve the super-soul even tolerably!

Just like the thread of surrender runs through and binds this superior narrative

So does the Lord permeate and connect the universe in a manner superlative

The fruit of learning the Ramayana is the inclination

For 'Sharanagati' and ultimate salvation.

Lava Kusha Narrate the Sri Ramayana

 

Valmiki composed the Sri Ramayana in several thousand verses

Of these, twenty four thousand stand, to annihilate earth's curses.

After Valmiki completed the epic, Lava and Kusha, the twin sons of Rama

Learnt to sing the Ramayana, while growing up in the sage's ‘ashrama.’

Rama had banished Sita to the forests when she was pregnant

She had taken refuge at Valmiki's hut, which was with divinity, vibrant.

 

At various places did Lava-Kusha sing the ballad pure

Whoever heard it shed tears of joy for sure.

One day a happy sage gave them a hearty blessing:

"The Hero of the epic will hear you sing."

The blessing came true when to Ayodhya the lads came

They were taken to the Royal assemblage where sat Rama of fame.

 

Rama saw the children and was struck by their appearance 

They looked like effulgent hermits and bold warriors at once

And when they embarked on singing His story

Sita's Husband became ecstatic, listening to Her glory.

As the Ramayana's verses flowed to music mellifluous 

The people gathered forgot Rama and began listening to His tale glorious.
